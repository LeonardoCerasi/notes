\section{Metric manifolds}

To import concepts of Geometry on smooth manifolds, it is necessary to define additional structure on them.

Consider an $ n $-dimensional $ \R $-vector space $ V $ and a symmetric tensor $ g \in T^0_2(V) $, i.e. such that $ g(v,w) = g(w,v) \,\,\forall v,w \in V $. Then, $ g $ is \bctxt{non-degenerate} if the linear map $ \hat{g} : V \ra V^* : \hat{g}(v)(w) = g(v,w) $ is an isomorphism, or equivalently if $ \forall v \in V : v \neq 0 \, \exists w \in V : g(v,w) \neq 0 $. With an adaptation of the Gram--Schmidt algorithm, every non-degenerate symmetric rank-$ (0,2) $ tensor can be transformed, by a change of basis, to one whose (local) matrix representation, i.e. $ [g_{ij}]_{i,j = 1 , \dots , n} $, is diagonal with all entries equal to $ \pm 1 $. By a result known as \emph{Sylvester's law of inertia}, the numbers $ r $ and $ s $ of negative and positive entries, respectively, are basis-independent, thus the ordered pair $ (r,s) $ is an invariant of $ g $, called its \bctxt{signature}.

\begin{definition}{Metric}{metric}
  Let $ \man $ be a smooth manifold. A symmetric tensor field $ g \in \tns{0}{2}(\man) $ is a \bcdef{metric} on $ \man $ if it is non-degenerate at each point $ p \in \man $ and if it has the same signature on all $ \man $. A smooth manifold equipped with a metric is a \bcdef{metric manifold}.
\end{definition}

The two most important cases for the signature of the metric tensor are $ (0,n) $, in which case it is a \emph{Riemannian metric}, and $ (1,n-1) $, which is a \emph{Lorentzian metric}. In general, any metric with $ r > 0 $ is called a pseudo-Riemannian metric.

\begin{observation}{Symmetric tensors}{symmetric-tensors}
  The expression of the metric in smooth chart $ (U,(x^i)) $ on $ \man $ does not make its symmetry clear:
  \begin{equation*}
    g = g_{ij} \dd x^i \otimes \dd x^j
  \end{equation*}
  To make the symmetry explicit, it is first necessary to further characterize symmetric tensors. Let $ V $ be an $ n $-dimensional $ \R $-vector space. A covariant $ k $-tensor is a rank-$ (0,k) $ tensor on $ V $, and it is said to be symmetric if its value is unchanged under the interchange of any pair of arguments: the set of symmetric covariant $ k $-tensors on $ V $ is denoted as $ \sv{k}(V) \ssq T^0_k(V) $. It is possible to define a natural projection $ \Sym : T^0_k(V) \ra \sv{k}(V) $ as:
  \begin{equation}
    (\Sym \alpha)(v_1 , \dots , v_k) \defeq \frac{1}{k!} \sum_{\sigma \in \sy{k}} \alpha(v_{\sigma(1)} , \dots , v_{\sigma(k)})
  \end{equation}
  which is clearly a symmetric $ k $-tensor. Note that $ \Sym \vert_{\sv{k}(V)} = \id_{\sv{k}(V)} $. In general, the tensor product of two symmetric tensors is not symmetric, but the symmetrization operator allows to define a \bcobs{symmetric product} of $ \alpha \in \sv{k}(V) $ and $ \beta \in \sv{l}(V) $:
  \begin{equation}
    \alpha \spr \beta \defeq \Sym(\alpha \otimes \beta)
  \end{equation}
  Clearly $ \alpha \spr \beta \in \sv{k+l}(V) $, since its explicit action is:
  \begin{equation}
    \alpha \spr \beta(v_1 , \dots , v_{k+l}) = \sum_{\sigma \in \sy{k+l}} \alpha(v_{\sigma(1)} , \dots , v_{\sigma(k)}) \beta(v_{\sigma(k+1)} \dots , v_{\sigma(k+l)})
  \end{equation}
  It can be shown that the symmetric product is symmetric and bilinear, and it has a particularly simple action on covectors $ \alpha , \beta \in V^* $:
  \begin{equation}
    \alpha \spr \beta = \frac{1}{2} (\alpha \otimes \beta + \beta \otimes \alpha)
  \end{equation}
  This last expression allows to make the symmetry of the metric transparent:
  \begin{equation*}
    g = g_{ij} \dd x^i \otimes \dd x^i = \frac{1}{2} (g_{ij} \dd x^i \otimes \dd x^j + g_{ji} \dd x^i \otimes \dd x^j) = \frac{1}{2} g_{ij} (\dd x^i \otimes \dd x^j + \dd x^j \otimes \dd x^i)
  \end{equation*}
  where $ g_{ij} = g_{ji} $ has been used. This is just the symmetric product of $ \dd x^i $ and $ \dd x^j $, hence:
  \begin{equation}
    g = g_{ij} \dd x^i \dd x^j
  \end{equation}
  which is now manifestly symmetric.
\end{observation}

\subsection{Riemannian manifolds}

Since the diagonal representation of a Riemannian metric is the identity matrix, $ g_p $ clearly defines an inner product on $ T_p\man $, since it is positive-definite: therefore, given $ v,w \in T_p\man $, the notation $ g_p(v,w) \equiv \braket{v,w}_g $ is set.

\begin{example}{Euclidean metric}{}
  The simplest example of a Riemannian metric is the Euclidean metric $ \bar{g} $ on $ \R^n $, which is expressed in standard coordinates as:
  \begin{equation*}
    \bar{g} = \delta_{ij} \dd x^i \dd x^j
  \end{equation*}
  It is common to abbreviate the symmetric product as $ \alpha \alpha \equiv \alpha^2 $, hence the Euclidean metric can be written as:
  \begin{equation*}
    \bar{g} = (\dd x^1)^2 + \dots + (\dd x^n)^2
  \end{equation*}
  Applied to $ v,w \in T_p\R^n $, it yields the Euclidean inner product:
  \begin{equation*}
    \bar{g}(v,w) = \delta_{ij} v^i w^j = \sum_{i = 1}^n v^i w^i \equiv v \cdot w
  \end{equation*}
\end{example}

A peculiar feature of Riemannian metrics, which is not present in pseudo-Riemannian metrics, is their abundance.

\begin{theorem}{Existence of Riemannian metric}{riemann-existence}
  Every smooth manifold admits a Riemannian metric.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proofbox}
  \begin{proof}
    Let $ \man $ be a smooth manifold with a smooth atlas $ \atl = \{(U_\alpha , \varphi_\alpha)\} $. In each coordinate domain there is a Riemannian metric given by the pull-back of the Euclidean metric $ g_\alpha = \varphi_\alpha^* \bar{g} $, whose coordinate expression is $ \delta_{ij} \dd x^i \dd x^j $. Then, consider a smooth partition of unity\footnotemark $ \{\psi_\alpha\} $ subordinate to the cover $ \{U_\alpha\} $, and define:
    \begin{equation*}
      g = \sum_\alpha \psi_\alpha g_\alpha
    \end{equation*}
    By local finiteness, this sum has only finitely many non-zero terms in the neighbourhood of each point $ p \in \man $, hence this expression defines a smooth tensor field on $ \man $. Since it is obviously symmetric, only positive-definiteness needs to be checked. Given any $ v \in T_p\man : v \neq 0 $:
    \begin{equation*}
      g_p(v,v) = \sum_\alpha \psi_\alpha(p) g_\alpha \vert_p(v,v)
    \end{equation*}
    This sum is non-negative, as each term is non-negative. Moreover, at least one function $ \psi_\alpha $ is strictly positive at $ p $ (as they must sum to $ 1 $), and $ g_\alpha \vert_p(v,v) > 0 $ since $ v \neq 0 $, therefore $ g_p(v,v) > 0 $.
  \end{proof}
\end{proofbox}

\footnotetext{Let $ \man $ be a topological space, and let $ \EuScript{X} = \{X_\alpha\}_{\alpha \in \mathcal{I}} $ be an arbitrary open cover of $ \man $. A \emph{partition of unity} subordinate to $ \EuScript{X} $ is a family $ \{\psi_\alpha\}_{\alpha \in \mathcal{I}} $ of continuous functions $ \psi_\alpha : \man \ra [0,1] \ssq \R $ such that:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \label{item:pu-1} $ \supp \psi_\alpha \ssq X_\alpha \,\,\forall \alpha \in \mathcal{I} $;
  \item \label{item:pu-2} the family $ \{\supp \psi_\alpha\}_{\alpha \in \mathcal{I}} $ is locally finite, i.e. every point has a neighbourhood that intersects $ \supp \psi_\alpha $ only for finitely many $ \alpha \in \mathcal{I} $;
  \item \label{item:pu-3} $ \sum_{\alpha \in \mathcal{I}} \psi_\alpha(p) = 1 \,\,\forall p \in \man $.
\end{enumerate}
The sum in \itemref{item:pu-3} has no convergence problems, since it has only a finite number of non-zero terms due to \itemref{item:pu-2}. If $ \man $ is a smooth manifold and each function $ \psi_\alpha $ is smooth, then this is a smooth partition of unity.}

This proof shows that there is a great amount of arbitrariness in the construction of a metric on a smooth manifold: indeed, the same manifold can be equipped with extremely different metrics, yielding extremely different geometric properties. The main geometric constructions induced by a Riemannian metric are:
\begin{itemize}
  \item the \bctxt{norm} of a tangent vector $ v \in T_p\man $:
    \begin{equation}
      \norm{v}_g \defeq \sqrt{\braket{v,v}_g}
    \end{equation}
  \item the \bctxt{angle} between two non-zero tangent vectors $ v,w \in T_p\man $, which is the unique $ \theta \in [0,\pi] $ such that:
    \begin{equation}
      \cos \theta \defeq \frac{\braket{v,w}_g}{\norm{v}_g \norm{w}_g}
    \end{equation}
  \item two tangent vectors $ v,w \in T_p\man $ are \bctxt{orthogonal} if $ \braket{v,w}_g = 0 $.
\end{itemize}

Note that two vectors are orthogonal either if one or both are zero or if the angle between them is $ \frac{\pi}{2} $.

\begin{definition}{Orthonormal frame}{}
  Let $ (\man,g) $ be a Riemannian manifold. A local frame $ (E_1 , \dots , E_n) $ on $ U \in \tp(\man) $ is an \bcdef{orthonormal frame} is $ (E_1\vert_p , \dots , E_n\vert_p) $ is an orthonormal basis of $ T_p\man $ at each point $ p \in U $, or equivalently if $ \braket{E_i , E_j}_g = \delta_{ij} $.
\end{definition}

In general, a smooth orthonormal frame can be found starting from a smooth local frame and applying the Gram--Schmidt algorithm: therefore, for each point $ p \in \man $, there is a smooth orthonormal frame on a neighbourhood of $ p $, found applying the Gram--Schmidt algorithm to the coordinate frame on said neighbourhood.

\subsubsection{Pull-back metrics}

Suppose $ \man_1 , \man_2 $ are smooth manifolds, $ g $ is a Riemannian metric on $ \man_2 $ and $ f : \man_1 \ra \man_2 $ is a smooth map. Then, the pull-back $ f^*g $ is a smooth covariant $ 2 $-tensor field on $ \man_1 $: if it is positive-definite, $ f^*g $ is a Riemannian metric on $ \man_1 $, called the \bctxt{pull-back} metric on $ \man_1 $ determined by $ f $.

\begin{example}{Helicoid}{}
  Consider the smooth map $ f : \R^2 \ra \R^3 $ given by:
  \begin{equation*}
    f(u,v) = (u \cos v , u \sin v , v)
  \end{equation*}
  whose image is a surface known as a \bcex{helicoid}. The pull-back metric $ f^*\bar{g} $ on the helicoid can be computed substituting the component functions of $ f $ in the coordinate expression of $ \bar{g} $:
  \begin{equation*}
    f^*\bar{g} = \dd (u \cos v)^2 + \dd (u \sin v)^2 + \dd v^2 = \dd u^2 + (1 + u^2) \dd v^2
  \end{equation*}
\end{example}

The same procedure can be applied to transform a Riemannian metric under a change of coordinates, which is thought as the identity map expressed in terms of different coordinates for the domain and the codomain.

Another important application of pull-back metrics is the study of geometrical invariants under isometries.

\begin{definition}{Riemannian isometry}{}
  Let $ (\man,g) $ and $ (\tilde{\man},\tilde{g}) $ be Riemannian manifolds. A diffeomorphism $ f : \man \ra \tilde{\man} $ is a \bcdef{Riemannian isometry} if $ f^* \tilde{g} = g $.
\end{definition}

This definition can be generalized to \emph{local Riemannian isometries}, where the condition of isometry is only enforced on some $ U \in \tp(\tilde{\man}) $. If there exists a Riemannian isometry between two Riemannian manifolds, they are said to be \emph{isometric}.

\subsubsection{Riemannian distance}

The introduction of a metric on a smooth manifold allows to measure the lengths of curves.

\begin{definition}{Length of curve}{}
  Let $ (\man,g) $ be a Riemannian manifold. If $ \gamma : [a,b] \ra \man $ is a piecewise-smooth curve, the \bcdef{length of $ \gamma $} is defined as:
  \begin{equation}
    \lng_g(\gamma) \defeq \int_a^b \dd t \norm{\gamma'(t)}_g
  \end{equation}
\end{definition}

This integral is well-defined since $ \norm{\gamma'(t)}_g $ is continuous on $ [a,b] $, except for finitely many values of $ t $. In general, given $ c \in (a,b) $:
\begin{equation}
  \lng_g(\gamma) = \lng_g(\gamma \vert_{[a,c]}) + \lng_g(\gamma \vert_{[c,b]})
\end{equation}
The lengths of curves are local isometry invariants of Riemannian manifolds: given a Riemannian isometry $ f : \man \ra \tilde{\man} $, for every piecewise-smooth curve $ \gamma $ on $ \man $ it is trivial to show that $ \lng_{\tilde{g}}(f \circ \gamma) = \lng_g(\gamma) $.

Moreover, the length of a curve is also invariant under reparametrizations of the curve. Given a piecewise-smooth curve $ \gamma : [a,b] \ra \man $ on a Riemannian manifold, a \bctxt{reparametrization} of $ \gamma $ induced by a diffeomorphism $ \varphi : [c,d] \ra [a,b] $ is a piecewise-smooth curve $ \tilde{\gamma} = \gamma \circ \varphi $.

\begin{proposition}{Reparametrization-invariance of length}{}
  Let $ (\man,g) $ be a Riemannian manifold, and let $ \gamma : [a,b] \ra \man $ be a piecewise-smooth curve. If $ \tilde{\gamma} $ is a reparametrization of $ \gamma $, then $ \lng_g(\tilde{\gamma}) = \lng_g(\gamma) $.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proofbox}
  \begin{proof}
    Assume first that $ \gamma $ is smooth, and let $ \varphi : [c,d] \ra [a,b] $ be a diffeomorphism such that $ \tilde{\gamma} = \gamma \circ \varphi $: since it is a diffeomorphism, either $ \varphi' > 0 $ or $ \varphi' < 0 $ everywhere. Inserting the reparametrization in the integral:
    \begin{equation*}
      \begin{split}
        \lng_g(\tilde{\gamma})
        & = \int_c^d \dd t \norm{\tilde{\gamma}'(t)}_g = \int_c^d \dd t \norm{\frac{\dd}{\dd t} (\gamma \circ \varphi)(t)}_g \\
        & = \int_c^d \dd t \norm{\varphi'(t) \gamma'(\varphi(t))}_g = \int_c^d \dd t \abs{\varphi'(t)} \norm{\gamma'(\varphi(t))}_g
      \end{split}
    \end{equation*}
    Now, if $ \varphi' > 0 $, then $ \abs{\varphi'(t)} = \varphi'(t) $ and, under the change of integration variable $ s = \varphi(t) $, the integral becomes:
    \begin{equation*}
      \lng_g(\tilde{\gamma}) = \int_a^b \dd s \norm{\gamma'(s)}_g = \lng_g(\gamma)
    \end{equation*}
    On the other hand, if $ \varphi' < 0 $, then $ \abs{\varphi'(t)} = - \varphi'(t) $, and the same transformation yields:
    \begin{equation*}
      \lng_g(\tilde{\gamma}) = - \int_d^c \dd s \norm{\gamma'(s)}_g = \lng_g(\gamma)
    \end{equation*}
    If $ \gamma $ is only piecewise-smooth, apply the same reasoning to each subinterval on which it is smooth.
  \end{proof}
\end{proofbox}

Having defined the notion of length for curves, it is then possible to introduce the notion of distance between points of the manifold. Consider a connected patch $ U \in \tp(\man) $ of a Riemannian manifold $ (U,g) $: for any $ p,q \in U $, the \bctxt{(Riemannian) distance} from $ p $ to $ q $ is defined as:
\begin{equation}
  \dist_g(p,q) \defeq \inf_\R \big\{ \lng_g(\gamma) \in \R^+_0 : \gamma : [a,b] \ra \man : \gamma(a) = p , \gamma(b) = q \big\}
\end{equation}
which is well-defined since $ p,q \in U $ connected. If $ \man $ is connected, the distance function is defined on the whole $ \man $, and in fact it gives $ \man $ the structure of metric space.

\begin{theorem}{Riemannian manifolds as metric spaces}{}
  Let $ (\man,g) $ be a connected Riemannian manifold. Then, $ (\man,\dist_g) $ is a metric space whose topology is the same as the original manifold topology.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proofbox}
  \begin{proof}
    By definition $ \dist_g(p,q) \leq 0 \,\,\forall p,q \in \man $. Since every constant curve has vanishing length, $ \dist_g(p,p) = 0 \,\,\forall p \in \man $, while $ \dist_g(p,q) = \dist_g(q,p) $ by reparametrization invariance.

    Now, suppose $ \gamma_1 $ and $ \gamma_2 $ are piecewise-smooth curves from $ p $ to $ q $ and from $ q $ to $ r $, respectively, and let $ \gamma $ be the piecewise-smooth curve that first follows $ \gamma_1 $ and then follows $ \gamma_2 $ (suitably reparametrized). Then:
    \begin{equation*}
      \dist_g(p,r) \leq \lng_g(\gamma) = \lng_g(\gamma_1) + \lng_g(\gamma_2)
    \end{equation*}
    Taking the infimum over all such $ \gamma_1 $ and $ \gamma_2 $, the triangle inequality $ \dist_g(p,r) \leq \dist_g(p,q) + \dist_g(q,r) $ is found.

    To prove that $ (\man,g) $ is a metric space, there only remains to show that $ \dist_g(p,q) > 0 $ for $ p \neq q \in \man $. To do so, let $ U $ be a smooth coordinate domain containing $ p $ and not $ q $, and identify it with $ U \in \tp(\R^n) $ through the corresponding smooth coordinate map.

    \begin{lemma}[borderline = {1pt}{0pt}{blue!35!black}]{}{norm-ineq}
      Let $ g $ be a Riemannian metric on $ U \in \tp(\R^n) $. Given a compact subset $ K \ssq U $, there exist $ c,C \in \R^+ $ such that:
      \begin{equation}
        c \norm{v}_{\bar{g}} \leq \norm{v}_g \leq C \norm{v}_{\bar{g}}
        \qquad
        \forall v \in T_x \R^n \, \forall x \in K
        \label{eq:norm-ineq}
      \end{equation}
    \end{lemma}

    \begin{proofbox}
      \begin{proof}
        For any compact subset $ K \ssq U $, let $ L \ssq T\R^n $ defined as:
        \begin{equation*}
          L = \{(x,y) \in T\R^n : x \in K , \norm{v}_g = 1\}
        \end{equation*}
        Given the canonical identification $ T\R^n \cong \R^n \times \R^n $, then $ L \cong K \times \Sn{n-1} $, hence it is compact. Since the norm is continuous and strictly positive over $ L $, there exist $ c,C \in \R^+ $ such that $ c \leq \norm{v}_g \leq C $ whenever $ (x,v) \in L $. If $ x \in K $ and $ v \neq 0 \in T_x\R^n $, let $ \lambda = \norm{v}_{\bar{g}} $, so that $ (x,\lambda^{-1}v) \in L $ and, by homogeneity of the norm:
        \begin{equation*}
          \norm{v}_g = \lambda \norm{\lambda^{-1} v}_g \leq \lambda C = C \norm{v}_{\bar{g}}
        \end{equation*}
        and similarly for the lower bound. The same inequalities become trivial for $ v = 0 $.
      \end{proof}
    \end{proofbox}

    Consider a regular coordinate ball $ V $ of radius $ \varepsilon > 0 $ and centered at $ p $ such that $ \overline{V} \ssq U $. Applying \lref{lemma:norm-ineq}, there are $ c,C \in \R^+ $ such that \eref{eq:norm-ineq} holds for all $ x \in \overline{V} $ and $ v \in T_x \man $. Then, for any piecewise-smooth curve $ \gamma $ lying entirely in $ \overline{V} $:
    \begin{equation*}
      c \lng_{\bar{g}}(\gamma) \leq \lng_g(\gamma) \leq C \lng_{\bar{g}}(\gamma)
    \end{equation*}
    Now, suppose $ \gamma : [a,b] \ra \man $ is a piecewise-smooth curve from $ p $ to $ q $, and let $ t_0 $ be the infimum of $ t \in [a,b] : \gamma(t) \notin \overline{V} $: by continuity $ \gamma(t_0) \in \pa V $ (indeed $ \pa V \cap \supp \gamma = \{t_0\} $), and $ \gamma(t) \in \overline{V} \,\,\forall t \in [a,t_0] $. Thus:
    \begin{equation*}
      \lng_g(\gamma) \geq \lng_g(\gamma \vert_{[a,t_0]}) \geq c \lng_{\bar{g}}(\gamma \vert_{[a,t_0]}) \geq c \dist_{\bar{g}}(p , \gamma(t_0)) = c \varepsilon > 0
    \end{equation*}
    Taking the infimum over all such curves, $ \dist_g(p,q) \geq c \varepsilon > 0 $ is concluded.

    Finally, there remains to show that the metric topology generated by $ \dist_g $ is the same as the given manifold topology on $ \man $. To do so, WTS $ U \in \tp_\text{man}(\man) \iff U \in \tp_\text{dist}(\man) $.

    Suppose fist that $ U \in \tp_\text{man}(\man) $, let $ p \in U $ and let $ V $ be a regular coordinate ball of radius $ \varepsilon > 0 $ centered at $ p $ suc that $ \overline{V} \ssq U $. The previous argument shows that $ \dist_g(p,q) \geq c \varepsilon $ for $ q \notin \overline{V} $, hence $ \dist_g(p,q) < c \varepsilon \implies q \in \overline{V} \ssq U $, i.e. the metric ball of radius $ c \varepsilon $ centered at $ p $ is contained in $ U $. Hence, $ U \in \tp_\text{dist}(\man) $.

    Conversely, suppose $ W \in \tp_\text{dist}(\man) $, and let $ p \in W $. Furthermore, let $ V $ be a regular coordinate ball of radius $ r > 0 $ centered at $ p $, let $ \bar{g} $ be the Euclidean metric on $ \overline{V} $ determined by the given coordinates, and let $ c , C \in \R^+ $ such that \eref{eq:norm-ineq} is satisfied for $ v \in T_q\man , q \in \overline{V} $. Consider $ \varepsilon \in (0,r) $ small enough that the metric ball centered at $ p $ of radius $ C \varepsilon $ is contained in $ W $, and let $ V_\varepsilon $ be the set of points in $ \overline{V} $ whose Euclidean distance from $ p $ is less than $ \varepsilon $. If $ q \in V_\varepsilon $, let $ \gamma $ be the straight-line segment in coordinates from $ p $ to $ q $. By \lref{lemma:norm-ineq} as above:
    \begin{equation*}
      \dist_g(p,q) \leq \lng_g(\gamma) \leq C \lng_{\bar{g}}(\gamma) \leq C \varepsilon
    \end{equation*}
    This shows that $ V_\varepsilon $ is contained in the metric ball of radius $ C \varepsilon $ centered at $ p $, and therefore in $ W $. Sinve $ V_\varepsilon \in \tp_\text{man}(\man) $ is a neighbourhood of $ p $, this shows that $ W \in \tp_\text{man}(\man) $.
  \end{proof}
\end{proofbox}

By this theorem, all of the terminology of metric spaces can be carried over to connected Riemannian manifolds. A connected Riemannian manifold $ (\man,g) $ is complete if $ (\man,\dist_g) $ is a complete metric space, i.e. if every Cauchy sequence in $ \man $ converges to a point in $ \man $, and $ g $ is then said to be a \bctxt{complete Riemannian metric}: it can be shown that every connected smooth manifold admits a complete Riemannian metric.

\subsection{Lorentzian manifolds}

The Lorentzian manifold is more subtle to study, since there is no analogue of \tref{th:riemann-existence} for Lorentzian metrics, a consequence of the fact that it is not generally true that a linear combination of non-degenerate covariant $ 2 $-tensors with positive coefficients is necessarily non-degenerate: indeed, not every smooth manifold admits a Lorentzian metric.

\begin{example}{Minkowski space}{}
  The simplest example of Lorentzian manifold is $ \R^n $ with the \bcex{Lorentz--Minkowski metric}:
  \begin{equation}
    \eta = - (\dd x^0)^2 + (\dd x^1)^2 + \dots + (\dd x^{n-1})^2
  \end{equation}
  Note that it is conventional to define the coordinate corresponding to the negative entry of the metric as the $ 0^\text{th} $ coordinate.
\end{example}

A Lorentzian metric does not induce naturally an inner product on tangent vectors, since the negative entry makes it non-positive-definite. Nonetheless, it is possible to define one on restricted classes of tangent vectors. To do so, classify $ v \in T_p\man $ as follows:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \bctxt{timelike} if $ g_p(v,v) < 0 $;
  \item \bctxt{lightlike} (or \emph{null}) if $ g_p(v,v) = 0 $;
  \item \bctxt{spacelike} if $ g_p(v,v) > 0 $.
\end{itemize}
Similarly, a \emph{timelike curve} is a curve whose tangent vector is everywhere timelike, and analogously for the other cases. Then, keeping the three classes of tangent vectors and curves separate, it is possible to import the same geometric constructions defined on Riemannian manifolds on spacelike vectors in a direct way, and on timelike vectors changing the sign of the inner product. Hence, for example, the length of a timelike piecewise-smooth curve $ \gamma : [a,b] \ra \man $ is defined as:
\begin{equation*}
  \lng_g(\gamma) \defeq \int_a^b \dd t \sqrt{- \braket{\gamma'(t) , \gamma'(t)}_g}
\end{equation*}
This is known as the \emph{proper time} of the timelike curve $ \gamma $ elapsed between $ \gamma(a) $ and $ \gamma(b) $. In general, the affine parameter of a timelike curve is its proper time. Indeed, defining the proper time of a timelike curve $ \gamma : [t_0,t_\text{f}] \ra \man $ as:
\begin{equation}
  \tau(t) \defeq \int_{t_0}^t \dd t \sqrt{-\braket{\gamma'(t) , \gamma'(t)}_g}
\end{equation}
then it is clear that $ \lng_g(\gamma) = \int_0^{\tau_\text{f}} \dd \tau $, with $ \tau_\text{f} \equiv \tau(t_\text{f}) $.

\begin{observation}{Negative Lorentzian metric}{}
  By \dref{def:metric}, if $ g $ is a metric on a smooth manifold $ \man $, then so is $ \tilde{g} = - g $. Obviously, the geometric description determined by $ \tilde{g} $ is completely equivalent to that of $ g $, provided that the sign change is accounted in all expressions involving an odd number of metric coefficients.

  In the Lorentzian case, the negative metric has the opposite signature of the ``ordinary" metric: this means that spacelike and timelike vectors are exchanged with each other, while null vectors remain unchanged.
\end{observation}

\subsection{Tangent-cotangent isomorphism}

The definition of a metric on a smooth manifold also provides a natural isomorphism between the tangent and cotangent bundles.

Let $ (\man,g) $ be a metric manifold, and define a bundle homomorphism (\dref{def:bundle-homomorphism}) $ \hat{g} : T\man \ra T^*\man $ as follows: for each $ p \in \man $ and $ v \in T_p\man $, let $ \hat{g}(v) \in T_p^*\man $ defined as:
\begin{equation}
  \hat{g}(v)(w) \defeq g_p(v,w)
  \qquad
  \forall w \in T_p\man
\end{equation}
To see that this is a smooth bundle homomorphism, consider its action on smooth vector fields: $ \hat{g}(X)(Y) = g(X,Y) $, with $ X,Y \in \xm $. Recall \eref{eq:tensor-linear}: as $ \hat{g}(X)(Y) $ is multilinear over $ \cm $, it is linear over $ \cm $ as a function of $ Y $, thus $ \hat{g}(X) $ is a smooth covector (i.e. rank-$ (0,1) $ tensor) field; then, since $ \hat{g}(X) $ is linear over $ \cm $ as a function of $ X $, $ \hat{g} $ is a smooth bundle homomorphism by \lref{lemma:char-bundle-homo}. Note that the same symbol is used for the pointwise bundle homomorphism $ \hat{g} : T\man \ra T^*\man $ and for the linear map on sections $ \hat{g} : \xm \ra \xms $.

Since the metric is non-degenerate, $ \hat{g}(v) = 0 \implies 0 = \hat{g}(v)(v) = \braket{v,v}_g \iff v = 0 \in T_p\man $, hence $ \ker \hat{g}\vert_{T_p\man} = 0 $, i.e. $ \hat{g} $ is injective. For dimensional reasons, therefore, $ \hat{g} $ is bijection, so it is a canonical smooth bundle isomorphism.

Given the canonical isomorphism $ T\man \cong T^*\man $, it is conventional to set a simpler notation through the so-called \emph{musical isomorphisms}:
\begin{equation*}
  T\man \ni X \xrightarrow{\ \hat{g} \ } X^\flat \equiv \hat{g}(X) \in T^*\man
  \qquad \qquad
  T^*\man \ni \omega \xrightarrow{\ \hat{g}^{-1}} \omega^\sharp \equiv \hat{g}^{-1}(\omega) \in T\man
\end{equation*}

In any smooth chart on $ \man $, the metric tensor is $ g = g_{ij} \dd x^i \dd x^j $, thus, given $ X,Y \in \xm $, the action of the bundle isomorphism is $ \hat{g}(X)(Y) = g_{ij} X^i X^j $, which implies that:
\begin{equation}
  X^\flat = \hat{g}(X) = g_{ij} X^i \dd x^j \equiv X_j \dd x^j
\end{equation}
It is then clear that the action of $ \hat{g} $ on a vector field lowers its contravariant index to a covariant one. The action of $ \hat{g} $ on $ \omega \in \xms $ is similar, since $ g_{ij} $ is invertible at each point (being the matrix representation of an isomorphism, which is invertible) with inverse denoted by $ g^{ij} $ (i.e. such that $ g_{ij} g^{jk} = g^{jk} g_{ij} = \delta^k_i $):
\begin{equation}
  \omega^\sharp = \hat{g}^{-1}(\omega) = g^{ij} \omega_i \frac{\pa}{\pa x^j} \equiv \omega^j \frac{\pa}{\pa x^j}
\end{equation}
Hence, the action of $ \hat{g}^{-1} $ on a covector field raises its covariant index to a contravariant one.

\begin{example}{Gradient operator}{}
  Given $ f \in \cm $, its \bcex{gradient} is defined as:
  \begin{equation}
    \grad f \defeq (\dd f)^\sharp
  \end{equation}
  Note that $ \grad f $ is the unique vector field such that, for any vector field $ X $ over $ \man $, satisfies:
  \begin{equation*}
    \braket{\grad f , X}_g = \hat{g}(\grad f)(X) = \dd f(X) = X(f)
  \end{equation*}
  In a smooth coordinate chart $ (U,(x^i)) $, the gradient has the expression:
  \begin{equation*}
    \grad f = g^{ij} \frac{\pa f}{\pa x^i} \frac{\pa}{\pa x^j}
  \end{equation*}
  which reduces to the usual expression in the Euclidean case.
\end{example}

\section{Differential forms}

\subsection{Grassmann algebra}

Let $ V $ be an $ n $-dimensional $ \R $-vector space. A covariant $ k $-tensor is said to be \emph{alternating} if its value changes sign under any interchange of two arguments, or equivalently if any permutation of its arguments results in an overall multiplication by the sign of said permutation. Alternating covariant $ k $-tensors are called \bctxt{exterior forms}, and they form a vector space $ \lv{k}(V) \ssq T^0_k(V) $.

\begin{lemma}{Characterization of alternating tensors}{cat}
  Let $ \alpha \in T^0_k(V) $. Then the following conditions are equivalent:
  \begin{enumerate}
    \item \label{item:cat-1} $ \alpha $ is alternating;
    \item \label{item:cat-2} $ \alpha(v_1 , \dots , v_k) = 0 $ whenever $ (v_1 , \dots , v_k) $ is linearly dependent;
    \item \label{item:cat-3} $ \alpha $ vanishes whenever two of its arguments are equal.
  \end{enumerate}
\end{lemma}

\begin{proofbox}
  \begin{proof}
    \itemref{item:cat-1} $ \implies $ \itemref{item:cat-3} and \itemref{item:cat-2} $ \implies $ \itemref{item:cat-3} trivially. WTS \itemref{item:cat-3} $ \implies $ \itemref{item:cat-1} and \itemref{item:cat-3} $ \implies $ \itemref{item:cat-2}.
    Assume that $ \alpha $ satisfies \itemref{item:cat-3}. For any $ k $-tuple $ (v_1 , \dots , v_k) $ of vectors, then:
    \begin{equation*}
      \begin{split}
        0
        & = \alpha(v_1 , \dots , v_i + v_j , \dots , v_i + v_j , \dots , v_k) \\
        & = \alpha(v_1 , \dots , v_i , \dots , v_i , \dots , v_k) + \alpha(v_1 , \dots , v_i , \dots , v_j , \dots , v_k) \\
        & \qquad \qquad + \alpha(v_1 , \dots , v_j , \dots , v_i , \dots , v_k) + \alpha(v_1 , \dots , v_j , \dots , v_j , \dots , v_k) \\
        & = \alpha(v_1 , \dots , v_i \dots , \dots , v_j , v_k) + \alpha(v_1 , \dots , v_j , \dots , v_i , \dots , v_k)
      \end{split}
    \end{equation*}
    Thus $ \alpha $ is alternating. On the other hand, if $ (v_1 , \dots , v_k) $ is linearly independent, then one vector, WLOG $ v_k $, can be written as a linear combination of the others, hence $ \alpha(v_1 , \dots , v_k) $ breaks down into a sum of vanishing terms by \itemref{item:cat-3}.
  \end{proof}
\end{proofbox}

Similarly to \obsref{obs:symmetric-tensors}, it is possible to define a natural projection $ \Alt : T^0_k(V) \ra \lv{k}(V) $ as:
\begin{equation}
  (\Alt \alpha)(v_1 , \dots , v_k) \defeq \frac{1}{k!} \sum_{\sigma \in \sy{k}} (\sgn \sigma) \alpha(v_{\sigma(1)} , \dots , v_{\sigma(k)})
\end{equation}
which is clearly an alternating $ k $-tensor. Note that $ \Alt \vert_{\lv{k}(V)} = \id_{\lv{k}(V)} $. For computations involving alternating tensors, it is convenient to define the following notation: given $ k \in \N $, an ordered $ k $-tuple $ I = (i_1 , \dots , i_k) \in \N^k $ is a \emph{multi-index of length $ k $}. Moreover, given $ \sigma \in \sy{n} $, the permutation of $ I $ under $ \sigma $ is denoted by $ I_\sigma \equiv (i_{\sigma(1)} , \dots , i_{\sigma(k)}) $, with the convention $ I_{\sigma \tau} \equiv (I_\sigma)_\tau $ for $ \sigma , \tau \in \sy{k} $.

Multi-indices allow to generalize the construction of the determinant in $ \R^n $. Let $ V $ be an $ n $-dimensional $ \R $-vector space, and suppose $ (\varepsilon^1 , \dots , \varepsilon^n) $ is a basis of $ V^* $. For each multi-index $ I = (i_1 , \dots , i_k) $ such that $ 1 \leq i_1 , \dots , i_k \leq n $, define $ \varepsilon^I \equiv \varepsilon^{i_1 \dots i_k} \in T^0_k(V) $ by:
\begin{equation}
  \varepsilon^I(v_1 , \dots , v_k) \defeq
  \begin{vmatrix}
    \varepsilon^{i_1}(v_1) & \dots & \varepsilon^{i_1}(v_k) \\
    \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\
    \varepsilon^{i_k}(v_1) & \dots & \varepsilon^{i_k}(v_k)
  \end{vmatrix}
  =
  \begin{vmatrix}
    v_1^{i_1} & \dots & v_k^{i_1} \\
    \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\
    v_1^{i_k} & \dots & v_k^{i_k}
  \end{vmatrix}
\end{equation}
Since the determinant changes sign under the interchange of any two columns, $ \varepsilon^I \in \lv{k}(V) $: this is called an \emph{elementary exterior form}.

\begin{lemma}{Properties of elementary exterior forms}{peef}
  Let $ V $ be an $ n $-dimensional $ \R $-vector space, and let $ (E_i) $ be a basis of $ V $. If $ (\varepsilon^i) $ is the dual basis of $ V^* $ and $ I = (i_1 , \dots , i_k) $ is a multi-index, then:
  \begin{itemize}
    \item \label{item:peef-1} if $ I $ has a repeated index, then $ \varepsilon^I \equiv 0 $;
    \item \label{item:peef-2} if $ J = I_\sigma $ for some $ \sigma \in \sy{k} $, then $ \varepsilon^I = (\sgn \sigma) \varepsilon^J $;
    \item \label{item:peef-3} defining the \bclemma{generalized Kronecker delta} as:
      \begin{equation}
        \delta^I_J \defeq
        \begin{vmatrix}
          \delta^{i_1}_{j_1} & \dots & \delta^{i_1}_{j_k} \\
          \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\
          \delta^{i_k}_{j_1} & \dots & \delta^{i_k}_{j_k}
        \end{vmatrix}
      \end{equation}
      then:
      \begin{equation}
        \varepsilon^I(E_{j_1} , \dots , E_{j_k}) = \delta^I_J
      \end{equation}
  \end{itemize}
\end{lemma}

\begin{proofbox}
  \begin{proof}
    If $ I $ has a repeated index, then $ \varepsilon^I $ vanishes since the determinant of a matrix with two equal columns is zero, proving \itemref{item:peef-1}. On the other hand, \itemref{item:peef-2} is trivial by the alternating property of the determinant. Finally, \itemref{item:peef-3} follows directly from the definition of $ \varepsilon^I $.
  \end{proof}
\end{proofbox}

Although $ \{\varepsilon^I\}_{I \in [0,n]^k} $ is not linearly independent by \itemref{item:peef-1}-\itemref{item:peef-2} of \lref{lemma:peef}, it is possible to define a basis of $ \lv{k}(V) $ choosing an appropriate subset of multi-indices.

\begin{proposition}{Basis of exterior forms}{ext-form-basis}
  Let $ V $ be an $ n $-dimensional $ \R $-vector space. If $ (\varepsilon^i) $ is a basis of $ V^* $, then for each $ k \in \N_{\leq n} $ a basis of $ \lv{k}(V) $ is found as:
  \begin{equation}
    \bas_k = \bigg\{ \varepsilon^I \in \lv{k}(V) : I = (i_1 , \dots , i_k) : 1 \leq i_1 < \dots < i_k \leq n \bigg\}
  \end{equation}
  Therefore $ \dim_\R \lv{k}(V) = \binom{n}{k} $.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proofbox}
  \begin{proof}
    The fact that $ \dim_\R \lv{k}(V) = 0 $ for $ k > n $ follows immediately from \itemref{item:cat-2} of \lref{lemma:cat}.

    In the $ k \leq n $ case, let $ (E^i) $ be the dual basis of $ V $. To show that $ \lv{k}(V) = \lspan_\R \bas_k $, let $ \alpha \in \lv{k}(V) $ and, for each arbitrary multi-index $ I = (i_1 , \dots , i_k) $, define a real number $ \alpha_I $ as:
    \begin{equation*}
      \alpha_I = \alpha(E_{i_1} , \dots , E_{i_k})
    \end{equation*}
    Since $ \alpha $ is alternating, by \lref{lemma:peef} $ \alpha_I = 0 $ if $ I $ contains a repeated index, and $ \alpha_J = (\sgn \sigma) \alpha_I $ if $ J = I_\sigma $ for some $ \sigma \in \sy{k} $. Therefore:
    \begin{equation*}
      \sum_{I : 1 \leq i_1 < \dots < i_k \leq n} \alpha_I \varepsilon^I(E_{j_1} , \dots , E_{j_k}) = \sum_{I : 1 \leq i_1 < \dots < i_k \leq n} \alpha_I \delta^I_J = \alpha_J = \alpha(E_{j_1} , \dots , E_{j_k})
    \end{equation*}
    Denoting the sum over \emph{increasing} multi-indices as $ {\sum_I}' $, this shows that $ {\sum_I}' \alpha_I \varepsilon^I = \alpha $, hence $ \lv{k}(V) = \lspan_\R \bas_k $.

    Then, to show that $ \bas_k $ is linearly independent, suppose that $ {\sum_I}' \alpha_I \varepsilon^I = 0 $ for some coefficients $ \alpha_I $, and let $ J $ be an increasing multi-index. Applying both sides to $ (E_{j_1} , \dots , E_{j_k}) $, by \lref{lemma:peef}:
    \begin{equation*}
      0 = {\sum_I}' \alpha_I \varepsilon^I(E_{j_1} , \dots , E_{j_k}) = \alpha_J
    \end{equation*}
    Thus, each coefficient $ \alpha_J $ vanishes, proving linear independence.
  \end{proof}
\end{proofbox}

\pref{prop:ext-form-basis} shows that there is a particular class of exterior forms: those with $ k = n $, known as \bctxt{top forms}. Indeed, $ \lv{n}(V) $ is $ 1 $-dimensional and spanned by $ \varepsilon^{1 \dots n} $: on $ \R^n $, this is just the determinant function $ e^{1 \dots n} $. Top forms behave simply under linear transformations. Consider a linear map $ T : V \ra V $, and defint $ \det T $ as the determinant of the representation matrix of $ T $ (in any basis, by the Binet theorem): then, $ \omega \in \lv{n}(V) $ transforms as:
\begin{equation}
  \omega(T v_1 , \dots , T v_n) = (\det T) \omega(v_1 , \dots , v_n)
  \label{eq:forms-determinant}
\end{equation}
Note that, like $ \sv{0}(V) = \R $ and $ \sv{1}(V) = V^* $, so $ \lv{0}(V) = \R $ and $ \lv{1}(V) = V^* $.

\subsubsection{Exterior product}

Analogously to the symmetric product of symmetric tensors $ \alpha \beta = \Sym (\alpha \otimes \beta) $, it is possible to define an alternating product of alternating tensors.

\begin{definition}{Exterior product}{}
  Let $ V $ an $ n $-dimensional $ \R $-vector space. Given $ \omega \in \lv{k}(V) $ and $ \eta \in \lv{l}(V) $, their \bcdef{exterior product} is the alternating tensor $ \omega \wedge \eta \in \lv{k+l}(V) $ defined as:
  \begin{equation}
    \omega \wedge \eta \defeq \frac{(k+l)!}{k!l!} \Alt(\omega \otimes \eta)
  \end{equation}
\end{definition}

The choice of the coefficient is motivated by the simple form taken by exterior product of elementary exterior forms. Given multi-indices $ I = (i_1 , \dots , i_k) $ and $ J = (j_1 , \dots , j_l) $, it can be shown that:
\begin{equation}
  \varepsilon^I \wedge \varepsilon^J = \varepsilon^{IJ}
  \label{eq:eef-prod}
\end{equation}
with $ IJ \equiv (i_1 , \dots , i_k , j_1 , \dots , j_l) $, i.e. a concatenation.

\begin{lemma}{Properties of the exterior product}{prop-exterior-product}
  Suppose $ \omega , \eta , \xi $ are exterior forms on a finite-dimensional $ \R $-vector space $ V $. Then:
  \begin{enumerate}
    \item \label{item:pwp-1} (bilinearity) for $ a , a' \in \R $:
      \begin{equation*}
        (a \omega + a' \eta ) \wedge \xi = a (\omega \wedge \xi) + a' (\eta \wedge \xi)
      \end{equation*}
      \begin{equation*}
        \xi \wedge (a \omega + a' \eta) = a (\xi \wedge \omega) + a' (\xi \wedge \eta)
      \end{equation*}
    \item \label{item:pwp-2} (associativity):
      \begin{equation*}
        \omega \wedge (\eta \wedge \xi) = (\omega \wedge \eta) \wedge \xi
      \end{equation*}
    \item \label{item:pwp-3} (anticommutativity) for $ \omega \in \lv{k}(V) $ and $ \eta \in \lv{l}(V) $:
      \begin{equation}
        \omega \wedge \eta = (-1)^{kl} \eta \wedge \omega
      \end{equation}
    \item \label{item:pwp-4} if $ (\varepsilon^i) $ is a basis of $ V^* $ and $ I = (i_1 , \dots , i_k) $ is a multi-index, then:
      \begin{equation}
        \varepsilon^I = \varepsilon^{i_1} \wedge \dots \wedge \varepsilon^{i_k}
      \end{equation}
    \item \label{item:pwp-5} for any $ \omega_1 , \dots , \omega_k \in V^* $ and $ v_1 , \dots , v_k \in V $:
      \begin{equation}
        \omega^1 \wedge \dots \wedge \omega^k (v_1 , \dots , v_k) = \det [\omega^j(v_i)]
      \end{equation}
  \end{enumerate}
\end{lemma}

\begin{proofbox}
  \begin{proof}
    Bilinearity follows immediately from definition, since the tensor product is bilinear and $ \Alt $ is linear. To prove associativity, use \eref{eq:eef-prod}:
    \begin{equation*}
      (\varepsilon^I \wedge \varepsilon^J) \wedge \varepsilon^K = \varepsilon^{IJK} = \varepsilon^I \wedge (\varepsilon^J \wedge \varepsilon^K)
    \end{equation*}
    and the general case following from multilinearity. Similarly, using \eref{eq:eef-prod} again:
    \begin{equation*}
      \varepsilon^I \wedge \varepsilon^J = \varepsilon^{IJ} = (\sgn \sigma) \varepsilon^{JI} = (\sgn \sigma) \varepsilon^J \wedge \varepsilon^I
    \end{equation*}
    where $ \sigma \in \sy{k+l} : IJ \mapsto JI $. Since $ \sgn \sigma = (-1)^{kl} $, as it can be decomposed into $ kl $ transpositions (each index of $ I $ must be moved past each of the indices of $ J $), anticommutativity is found from multilinearity. Finally, \itemref{item:pwp-4} follows by induction from \eref{eq:eef-prod}, while to prove \itemref{item:pwp-5} note that, when $ \omega^j = \varepsilon^{i_j} $, it reduces to \itemref{item:pwp-4}, and then use multilinearity.
  \end{proof}
\end{proofbox}

In general, an exterior $ k $-form is said to be decomposable if it can be written as the exterior product of $ k $ exterior $ 1 $-forms. Note that, although not every exterior $ k $-form is decomposable when $ k > 1 $, every exterior $ k $-form can be written as a linear combination of decomposable exterior $ k $-forms (elementary exterior $ k $-forms).

Since it can be shown that the exterior product is the unique associate bilinear anticommutative map $ \lv{k}(V) \times \lv{l}(V) \ra \lv{k+l}(V) $, the ensemble of $ \lv{k}(V) $ spaces form a natural structure on $ V $.

\begin{definition}{Grassmann algebra}{}
  Let $ V $ be an $ n $-dimensional $ \R $-vector space. The \bcdef{Grassmann algebra} (or \emph{exterior algebra}) of $ V $ is defined as:
  \begin{equation}
    \ga(V) \defeq \bigoplus_{k = 0}^n \lv{k}(V)
  \end{equation}
\end{definition}

By \pref{prop:ext-form-basis}, it follows that $ \dim_\R \ga(V) = 2^n $. The Grassmann algebra has the structure of $ 2^n $-dimensional $ \R $-vector space and of anticommutative $ \N_0 $-graded\footnote{Given an index set $ \mathcal{I} $, a $ \K $-vector space $ V $ is said to be \emph{$ \mathcal{I} $-graded} if there exists a family of subspaces $ \{V_i\}_{i \in \mathcal{I}} $ such that $ V = \bigoplus_{i \in \mathcal{I}} V_i $.} unital associative algebra\footnotemark.

\footnotetext{An $ n $-dimensional \emph{associative $ \K $-algebra} is an $ n $-dimensional $ \K $-vector space $ \mathcal{A} $ equipped with a bilinear map $ \mathcal{A} \times \mathcal{A} \ni (a,b) \mapsto ab \in \mathcal{A} $ such that:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item $ (ab)c = a(cb) \,\,\forall a,b,c \in \mathcal{A} $;
  \item $ a (b + c) = ab + ac \land (a + b) c = ac + ab \,\,\forall a,b,c \in \mathcal{A} $;
  \item $ \lambda (ab) = (\lambda a) b = a (\lambda b) \,\,\forall \lambda \in \K , a,b \in \mathcal{A} $.
\end{enumerate}
An algebra is said to be \emph{unital} if $ \exists \emph{1} \in \mathcal{A} : \emph{1} a = a \emph{1} = a \,\,\forall a \in \mathcal{A} $. Moreover, given an index set $ \mathcal{I} $, an algebra $ \mathcal{A} $ is $ \mathcal{I} $-graded if $ \mathcal{A} $ is an $ \mathcal{I} $-graded vector space and the product satisfies $ (\mathcal{A}_k) (\mathcal{A}_l) \ssq \mathcal{A}_{k+l} \,\,\forall k,l \in \mathcal{I} $, and it is anticommutative if $ ab = (-1)^{kl} ba \,\,\forall a \in \mathcal{A}_k , b \in \mathcal{A}_l $.}

\subsubsection{Interior product}

While the exterior products allows to build higher-rank exterior forms from lower-rank one, there is another operation which has the opposite effect, resulting in a lower-rank exterior form from a higher-rank one.

\begin{definition}{Interior product}{}
  Let $ V $ be an $ n $-dimensional $ \R $-vector space. For each $ v \in V $, the linear map $ \ip_v : \lv{k}(V) \ra \lv{k-1}(V) $, called the \bcdef{interior product} with $ v $, is defined as:
  \begin{equation}
    \ip_v \omega(w_1 , \dots , w_{k-1}) \defeq \omega(v , w_1 , \dots , w_{k-1})
  \end{equation}
\end{definition}

Therefore, $ \ip_v \omega $ is obtained inserting $ v $ as the first argument of $ \omega $. By convention, $ \ip_v \vert_{\lv{0}(V)} \equiv 0 $.

\begin{lemma}{Properties of the interior product}{}
  Let $ V $ be an $ n $-dimensional $ \R $-vector space and $ v \in V $. Then:
  \begin{enumerate}
    \item \label{item:pip-1} $ \ip_v \circ \ip_v = 0 $;
    \item \label{item:pip-2} if $ \omega \in \lv{k}(V) $ and $ \eta \in \lv{l}(V) $, then:
      \begin{equation}
        \ip_v(\omega \wedge \eta) = (\ip_v \omega) \wedge \eta + (-1)^k \omega \wedge (\ip_v \eta)
      \end{equation}
  \end{enumerate}
\end{lemma}

\begin{proofbox}
  \begin{proof}
    For exterior $ k $-forms with $ k \geq 2 $, \itemref{item:pip-1} is immediate from definition by the alternating nature of exterior forms, while for $ k = 1,0 $ it follows from the convention $ \ip_v \vert_{\lv{0}(V)} \equiv 0 $.

    To prove \itemref{item:pip-2}, it suffices to consider the case of $ \omega , \eta $ decomposable, since every exterior $ k $-form can be written as a linear combination of decomposable ones. For general $ \omega^1 , \dots , \omega^k \in \lv{1}(V) $ WTS:
    \begin{equation}
      \ip_v(\omega^1 \wedge \dots \wedge \omega^k) = \sum_{i = 1}^k (-1)^{i-1} \omega^i(v) \omega^1 \wedge \dots \wedge \omega^{i-1} \wedge \omega^{i+1} \wedge \dots \wedge \omega^k
    \end{equation}
    which clearly implies \itemref{item:pip-2}. To prove this equation, write $ v_1 \equiv v $ and apply both sides to the $ (k-1) $-tuple of vectors $ (v_2 , \dots , v_k) $:
    \begin{multline*}
      (\omega^1 \wedge \dots \wedge \omega^k)(v_1 , \dots , v_k) \\
      = \sum_{i = 1}^k (-1)^{i-1} \omega^i(v_1) (\omega^1 \wedge \dots \wedge \omega^{i-1} \wedge \omega^{i+1} \wedge \dots \wedge \omega^k)(v_2 , \dots , v_k)
    \end{multline*}
    The left-hand side is just $ \det [\omega^i(v_j)] \equiv \det \mathrm{v} $. Now, define $ \mathrm{v}^i_j $ as the $ (k-1) \times (k-1) $ submatrix of $ \mathrm{v} $ obtained removing the $ i^\text{th} $ row and the $ j^\text{th} $ column. Then, this equation becomes:
    \begin{equation*}
      \det \mathrm{v} = \sum_{i = 1}^k (-1)^{i-1} \omega^i(v_1) \det \mathrm{v}^i_1
    \end{equation*}
    which is just the Laplace expansion of $ \det \mathrm{v} $ by minors along the first column, hence it is an identity.
  \end{proof}
\end{proofbox}

\subsection{Differential forms}

Consider an $ n $-dimensional smooth manifold. It is possible to define the subset of $ T^0_k\man $ of alternating rank-$ k $ covariant tensors on $ \man $ as:
\begin{equation}
  \lm{k} \defeq \bigsqcup_{p \in \man} \lv{k}(T_p\man)
\end{equation}
It can be shown that $ \lm{k} $ is a smooth rank-$ \binom{n}{k} $ vector subbundle of $ T^0_k\man $. Sections of $ \lm{k} $ are called \bctxt{differential $ k $-forms} (or just $ k $-forms): therefore, a $ k $-form is a (continuous) tensor field whose value at each point is an alternating tensor. The $ \R $-vector space of smooth $ k $-forms is denoted as:
\begin{equation}
  \df{k} \defeq \Gamma(\lm{k})
\end{equation}
The wedge product is extended to $ k $-forms in a pointwise manner, i.e. $ (\omega \wedge \eta)_p = \omega_p \wedge \eta_p $. Then, it is possible to define the following direct product:
\begin{equation}
  \dft \defeq \bigoplus_{k = 0}^n \df{k}
\end{equation}
which, equipped with the wedge product, is an anticommutative $ \N_0 $-graded associative unital algebra. In any smooth chart $ (U,(x^i)) $, a $ k $-form $ \omega $ can be written locally as:
\begin{equation*}
  \omega = \frac{1}{k!} \omega_{i_1 \dots i_k} \dd x^{i_1} \wedge \dots \wedge \dd x^{i_k} = {\sum_I}' \omega_I \dd x^I
\end{equation*}
where the coefficients $ \omega_I $ are continuous functions defined on the coordinate domain, and $ \dd x^I \equiv \dd x^{i_1} \wedge \dots \wedge \dd x^{i_k} $. Using \lref{lemma:peef}, the component functions are found as:
\begin{equation*}
  \omega_I = \omega\left( \frac{\pa}{\pa x^{i_1}} , \dots , \frac{\pa}{\pa x^{i_k}} \right)
\end{equation*}
If $ f : \man_1 \ra \man_2 $ is a smooth map and $ \omega $ is a $ k $-form on $ \man_2 $, its pull-back on $ \man_1 $ is given by \eref{eq:tens-pb}:
\begin{equation*}
  (f^*\omega)_p(v_1 , \dots , v_k) = \omega_{f(p)}(\dd f_p(v_1) , \dots , \dd f_p(v_k))
\end{equation*}

\begin{lemma}{Pull-back of differential forms}{pull-back-diff-forms}
  Let $ f : \man_1 \ra \man_2 $ be a smooth map. Then:
  \begin{enumerate}
    \item \label{item:pbdf-1} $ f^* : \dfm{k}(\man_2) \ra \dfm{k}(\man_1) $ is linear over $ \R $;
    \item \label{item:pbdf-2} $ f^*(\omega \wedge \eta) = (f^*\omega) \wedge (f^*\eta) $;
    \item \label{item:pbdf-3} in any smooth chart $ (U,(y^i)) $ of $ \man_2 $:
      \begin{equation}
        f^*\left( \frac{1}{k!} \omega_{i_1 \dots i_k} \dd y^{i_1} \wedge \dots \wedge \dd y^{i_k} \right) = \frac{1}{k!} (\omega_{i_1 \dots i_k} \circ f) \dd (y^{i_1} \circ f) \wedge \dots \wedge \dd (y^{i_k} \circ f)
      \end{equation}
  \end{enumerate}
\end{lemma}

Hence, the pull-back of differential forms is computed similarly to that of arbitrary tensor fields.

\begin{proposition}{Pull-back of top forms}{pull-back-top-forms}
  Let $ f : \man_1 \ra \man_2 $ be a smooth map between smooth $ n $-manifolds. If $ (U,(x^i)) $ and $ (V,(y^i)) $ are smooth charts on $ \man_1 $ and $ \man_2 $, respectively, and $ u $ is a continuous function $ u : \man_2 \ra \R $, then on $ U \cap f^{-1}(V) $:
  \begin{equation}
    f^*(u \, \dd y^1 \wedge \dots \wedge \dd y^n) = (u \circ f) (\det \jac{f}) \dd x^1 \wedge \dots \wedge \dd x^n
    \label{eq:jacobian}
  \end{equation}
  where $ \jac{f} $ is the Jacobian matrix of $ f $ in these coordinates.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proofbox}
  \begin{proof}
    Since the fiber of $ \lm{n} $ is spanned by $ \dd x^1 \wedge \dots \wedge \dd x^n $ at each point, it suffices to show that both sides of \eref{eq:jacobian} give the same result when applied to $ (\pa_1 , \dots , \pa_n) $. From \lref{lemma:pull-back-diff-forms}:
    \begin{equation*}
      f^*(u \, \dd y^1 \wedge \dots \wedge \dd y^n) = (u \circ f) \dd f^1 \wedge \dots \wedge \dd f^n
    \end{equation*}
    Then, using \itemref{item:pwp-5} of \lref{lemma:prop-exterior-product}:
    \begin{equation*}
      \dd f^1 \wedge \dots \wedge \dd f^n \left( \frac{\pa}{\pa x^1} , \dots , \frac{\pa}{\pa x^n} \right) = \det \left[ \dd f^j \left( \frac{\pa}{\pa x^i} \right) \right] = \det \jac{f}
    \end{equation*}
    The left-hand side then gives $ (u \circ f) \det \jac{f} $, which is equal to the right-hand side since $ \dd x^1 \wedge \dots \wedge \dd x^n (\pa_1 , \dots , \pa_n) = 1 $.
  \end{proof}
\end{proofbox}

An obvious application of \pref{prop:pull-back-top-forms} is to coordinate changes. Indeed, given two overlapping smooth charts $ (U,(x^i)) $ and $ (\tilde{U},\tilde{x}^i) $ on a smooth $ n $-manifold $ \man $, then on $ U \cap \tilde{U} $:
\begin{equation*}
  \dd \tilde{x}^1 \wedge \dots \wedge \dd \tilde{x}^n = \det \left[ \frac{\pa \tilde{x}^j}{\pa x^i} \right] \dd x^1 \wedge \dots \wedge \dd x^n
\end{equation*}

The interior product too extends naturally to vector fields and differential forms by letting it act pointwise. If $ X \in \xm $ and $ \omega \in \df{k} $, then $ \ip_X \omega \in \df{k-1} $ is defined by $ (\ip_X \omega)_p = \ip_{X_p} \omega_p $: therefore, $ \ip_X : \df{k} \ra \df{k-1} $ is linear over $ \cm $ and defined a smooth bundle homomorphism $ \ip_X : \lm{k} \ra \lm{k-1} $.

\subsubsection{Exterior derivative}

It is possible to define a natural differential operator on smooth forms, which generalizes the differential of a function, but first it is necessary to analyze the Euclidean case.

If $ \omega = {\sum_J}' \omega_J \dd x^J $ is a smooth $ k $-form on $ U \in \tp(\R^n) $, its \bctxt{exterior derivative} $ \dd \omega $ is the $ (k+1) $-form defined as:
\begin{equation}
  \dd \omega \defeq {\sum_J}' \dd \omega_J \wedge \dd x^J = {\sum_J}' \sum_{i = 1}^n \frac{\pa \omega_J}{\pa x^i} \dd x^i \wedge \dd x^J
\end{equation}
where $ \dd \omega_J $ is the differential of the component function $ \omega_J $. On $ \R $-valued functions ($ 0 $-forms), the exterior derivative reduces to the differential.

\begin{lemma}[before upper = {\tcbtitle}]{Properties ot the Euclidean exterior derivative}{peed}
  \begin{enumerate}
    \item \label{item:peed-1} $ \dd $ is linear over $ \R $;
    \item \label{item:peed-2} if $ \omega \in \dfm{k}(U) $ and $ \eta \in \dfm{l}(U) $, with $ U \in \tp(\R^n) $, then:
      \begin{equation}
        \dd (\omega \wedge \eta) = \dd \omega \wedge \eta + (-1)^k \omega \wedge \dd \eta
      \end{equation}
    \item \label{item:peed-3} $ \dd \circ \dd \equiv 0 $;
    \item \label{item:peed-4} $ \dd $ commutes with pull-backs, i.e. given a smooth map $ f : U \ra V $, with $ U \in \tp(\R^n) $ and $ V \in \tp(\R^m) $, and $ \omega \in \dfm{k}(V) $, then:
      \begin{equation}
        f^*(\dd \omega) = \dd (f^* \omega)
      \end{equation}
  \end{enumerate}
\end{lemma}

\begin{proofbox}
  \begin{proof}
    Linearity over $ \R $ is trivial from definition. To prove \itemref{item:peed-2}, by linearity it suffices to consider $ \omega = u \, \dd x^I \in \dfm{k}(U) $ and $ \eta = v \, \dd x^J \in \dfm{l}(U) $, for smooth functions $ u $ and $ v $:
    \begin{equation*}
      \begin{split}
        \dd (\omega \wedge \eta)
        & = \dd (u v \, \dd x^I \wedge \dd x^J) = (u \, \dd v + v \, \dd u) \wedge \dd x^I \wedge \dd x^J \\
        & = (\dd u \wedge \dd x^I) \wedge (v \, \dd x^J) + (-1)^k (u \, \dd x^I) \wedge (\dd v \wedge \dd x^J)
      \end{split}
    \end{equation*}
    where the $ (-1)^k $ comes from $ \dd v \wedge \dd x^I = (-1)^k \dd x^I \wedge \dd v $, since $ \dd x^I $ is a $ k $-form and $ \dd v $ is a $ 1 $-form.
    WTS $ \dd (u \, \dd x^I) = \dd u \wedge \dd x^I $ for any multi-index $ I $, not just increasing ones: if $ I $ has repeated indices, then $ \dd (u \, \dd x^I) = 0 = \dd u \wedge \dd x^I $, otherwise let $ \sigma $ be a permutation of $ I $ which sends it to an increasing multi-index $ J $, so that:
    \begin{equation*}
      \dd (u \, \dd x^I) = (\sgn \sigma) \dd (u \, \dd x^I) = (\sgn \sigma) \dd u \wedge \dd x^J = \dd u \wedge \dd x^I
    \end{equation*}
    Inserting this into the above expression yields \itemref{item:peed-2}. Now, consider \itemref{item:peed-3} in the case of a $ 0 $-form:
    \begin{equation*}
      \dd (\dd u) = \dd \left( \frac{\pa u}{\pa x^i} \dd x^i \right) = \frac{\pa u^2}{\pa x^i \pa x^j} \dd x^i \wedge \dd x^j = 0
    \end{equation*}
    which vanishes since the first factor is symmetric under $ i \lra j $ by the Schwartz lemma on smooth functions $ \pa_i \pa_j u = \pa_j \pa_i u $, while the second factor is antisymmetric under $ i \lra j $ by definition. In the general case:
    \begin{equation*}
      \dd (\dd \omega) = \dd {\sum_J}' \dd \omega_J \wedge \dd x^J = {\sum_J}' \dd (\dd \omega_J) \wedge \dd x^J + {\sum_J}' \sum_{i = 1}^k (-1)^i \dd x^{j_1} \wedge \dots \wedge \dd (\dd x^{j_i}) \wedge \dots \wedge \dd x^{j_k}
    \end{equation*}
    The first term vanishes since $ \omega_J $ is a $ 0 $-form, while the second vanishes as $ \pa_i 1 = 0 $. Finally, to prove \itemref{item:peed-4}, again it suffices to consider $ \omega = u \, \dd x^{i_1} \wedge \dots \wedge \dd x^{i_k} $:
    \begin{equation*}
      f^*(\dd (u \, \dd x^I)) = f^*(\dd u \wedge \dd x^I) = \dd (u \circ f) \wedge \dd (x^{i_1} \circ f) \wedge \dots \wedge \dd (x^{i_k} \circ f)
    \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*}
      \dd (f^*(u \, \dd x^I)) = \dd ((u \circ f) \dd (x^{i_1} \circ f) \wedge \dots \wedge \dd (x^{i_k} \circ f))
    \end{equation*}
    which are equal.
  \end{proof}
\end{proofbox}

Usually $ \dd \circ \dd \equiv \dd^2 $.
It is now possible to extend the definition of exterior derivative to manifolds.

\begin{theorem}{Existence and uniqueness of exterior derivative}{}
  Let $ \man $ be a smooth $ n $-manifold. There exists a unique operator $ \dd : \df{k} \ra \df{k+1} $ for all $ k = 0, \dots, n-1 $, called \bcth{exterior derivative}, which satisfies:
  \begin{enumerate}
    \item \label{item:eued-1} $ \dd $ is linear over $ \R $;
    \item \label{item:eued-2} if $ \omega \in \df{k} $ and $ \eta \in \df{l} $, then:
      \begin{equation}
        \dd (\omega \wedge \eta) = \dd \omega \wedge \eta + (-1)^k \omega \wedge \dd \eta
      \end{equation}
    \item \label{item:eued-3} $ \dd^2 \equiv 0 $;
    \item \label{item:eued-4} given $ f \in \cm = \df{0} $, then $ \dd f $ is the differential of $ f $, defined by $ \dd f(X) = X(f) $;
    \item \label{item:eued-5} in any smooth chart $ (U,(x^i)) $ on $ \man $, given $ \omega \in \df{k} $:
      \begin{equation}
        \dd \omega = {\sum_J}' \dd \omega_J \wedge \dd x^J
        \label{eq:exterior-derivative-def}
      \end{equation}
  \end{enumerate}
\end{theorem}

\begin{proofbox}
  \begin{proof}
    To prove existence, suppose $ \omega \in \df{k} $, and define $ \dd \omega $ by \eref{eq:exterior-derivative-def} in each smooth chart on $ \man $. More precisely, in each smooth chart $ (U,\varphi) $ on $ \man $, set:
    \begin{equation*}
      \dd \omega = \varphi^* \dd ((\varphi^{-1})^* \omega)
    \end{equation*}
    To see that this is well-defined, note that, for any other smooth chart $ (V,\psi) $, the map $ \varphi \circ \psi^{-1} $ is a diffeomorphism $ V \ra U $, hence by \itemref{item:peed-4} of \lref{lemma:peed}:
    \begin{equation*}
      (\varphi \circ \psi^{-1})^* \dd ((\varphi^{-1})^* \omega) = \dd ((\varphi \circ \psi^{-1})^* (\varphi^{-1})^* \omega)
    \end{equation*}
    Together with $ (\varphi \circ \psi^{-1})^* = (\psi^{-1})^* \circ \varphi^* $, this implies $ \varphi^* \dd ((\varphi^{-1})^* \omega) = \psi^* \dd ((\psi^{-1})^* \omega) $, so $ \dd \omega $ is well-defined, and it satisfies \iitemref{item:eued-1}{item:eued-4} by \pref{lemma:peed}.

    To prove uniqueness, suppose $ \dd $ is any operator satisfying \iitemref{item:eued-1}{item:eued-4}. It can be proved that $ \dd \omega $ is determined locally, i.e. if $ \omega_1 $ and $ \omega_2 $ are $ k $-forms which agree on $ U \in \tp(\man) $, then $ \dd \omega_1 = \dd \omega_2 $ on $ U $. With this, let $ \omega \in \df{k} $ arbitrary, and let $ (U,\varphi) $ be any smooth coordinate chart on $ \man $: then, $ \omega $ can be written on $ U $ as $ \omega = {\sum_I}' \omega_I \dd x^I $. For any $ p \in U $, it is possible to construct global smooth functions $ \tilde{\omega}_I $ and $ \tilde{x}^i $ on $ \man $ which agree with $ \omega_I $ and $ x^i $ in a neighbourhood of $ p $. Using \iitemref{item:eued-1}{item:eued-4} and the preceding observation, it follows that $ \dd $ is given by \eref{eq:exterior-derivative-def} at $ p $. Since $ p $ is arbitrary, then this $ \dd $ must be equal to the one defined above.
  \end{proof}
\end{proofbox}

If $ \mathcal{A} = \bigoplus_{k \in \mathcal{I}} \mathcal{A}_k $ is an $ \mathcal{I} $-graded algebra, a linear map $ T : \mathcal{A} \ra \mathcal{A} $ is a \emph{map of degree $ m $} if $ T(\mathcal{A}_k) \ssq \mathcal{A}_{m+k} \,\,\forall k \in \mathcal{I} $, and it is an \emph{antiderivation} if $ T(xy) = T(x) y+ (-1)^k x T(y) \,\,\forall x \in \mathcal{A}_k , y \in \mathcal{A}_l $. Therefore, in the language of graded algebras, $ \dd $ is an antiderivation on $ \dft $ of degree $ +1 $ whose square is zero, while $ \ip_X $ is an antiderivation of $ \dft $ of degree $ -1 $ whose square is zero, for $ X \in \xm $.

Note that \itemref{item:peed-4} of \lref{lemma:peed} holds for the general exterior derivative too. If $ f : \man_1 \ra \man_2 $ is a smooth map, then for each $ k $ the pull-back map $ f^* : \dfm{k}(\man_2) \ra \dfm{k}(\man_1) $ commutes with $ \dd $, i.e. for all $ \omega \in \dfm{k}(\man_2) $:
\begin{equation}
  f^*(\dd \omega) = \dd (f^* \omega)
\end{equation}

The exterior derivative can be thought, in a certain sense, as the dual of the Lie bracket, as knowing all the Lie brackets of basis vector fields in a smooth local frame allows to compute the exterior derivatives of dual covector fields, and vice versa.

\begin{proposition}{Duality between exterior derivative and Lie brackets}{duality-edlb}
  Let $ \man $ be a smooth $ n $-manifold, let $ (E_i) $ be a smooth local frame on $ \man $ and let $ (\varepsilon^i) $ be its dual coframe. For each $ i = 1 , \dots  , n $, let $ b^i_{jk} $ and $ c^i_{jk} $ be defined so that:
  \begin{equation*}
    \dd \varepsilon^i = \sum_{j < k = 1}^n b^i_{jk} \varepsilon^j \wedge \varepsilon^k
    \qquad \qquad
    [E_j , E_k] = c^i_{jk} E_i
  \end{equation*}
  Then, $ b^i_{jk} = - c^i_{jk} $.
\end{proposition}

With \pref{prop:duality-edlb}, it is possible to prove an invariant expression for the exterior derivative of $ \omega \in \df{k} $, in addition to the coordinate formula \eref{eq:exterior-derivative-def}: for any $ X_1 , \dots , X_{k+1} \in \xm $:
\begin{multline}
  \dd \omega(X_1 , \dots , X_{k+1}) = \sum_{1 \leq i \leq k+1} (-1)^{i-1} X_i(\omega(X_1 , \dots , X_{i-1} , X_{i+1} , \dots , X_{k+1})) + \\
  + \sum_{1 \leq i < j \leq k+1} (-1)^{i+j} \omega([X_i , X_j] , X_1 , \dots , X_{i-1} , X_{i+1} , \dots , X_{j-1} , X_{j+1} , \dots , X_{k+1})
\end{multline}
The restriction of this formula to $ 1 $-forms is particularly useful:
\begin{equation}
  \dd \omega(X,Y) = X(\omega(Y)) - Y(\omega(X)) - \omega([X,Y])
\end{equation}

\subsubsection{Lie derivatives of differential forms}

\eref{eq:tensor-lie-der} applies to differential forms too. However, the Lie derivative takes a particularly simple form on $ \dft $ due to the exterior derivative.

\begin{theorem}{Cartan's theorem}{}
  Let $ \man $ be a smooth $ n $-manifold. Then, for any $ V \in \xm $ and $ \omega \in \dft $:
  \begin{equation}
    \ld_V \omega = (\ip_V \circ \dd + \dd \circ \ip_V) \omega
    \label{eq:cartan-formula}
  \end{equation}
\end{theorem}

\begin{proofbox}
  \begin{proof}
    To show the thesis for $ \df{k} $, with $ k = 0 , \dots , n $, use induction on $ k $. First, consider a $ 0 $-form $ f \in \cm $:
    \begin{equation*}
      \ip_V (\dd f) + \dd (\ip_V f) = \ip_V \dd f = \dd f(V) = V(f) = \ld_V f
    \end{equation*}
    Now, let $ k \geq 1 $, and suppose that \eref{eq:cartan-formula} holds for all forms of degree less than $ k $. Let $ \omega \in \df{k} $ arbitrary, which in smooth local coordinates is $ \omega = {\sum_I}' \omega_I \dd x^I $, and write e.g. $ u = x^{i_1} $ and $ \beta = \omega_I \dd x^{i_2} \wedge \dots \wedge \dd x^{i_k} $. Then, it is clear that each term of the sum can be written as $ \dd u \wedge \beta $, for some $ u \in \cm $ and $ \beta \in \df{k-1} $.

    \begin{lemma}{}{prod-rule-forms}
      Let $ \man $ be a smooth manifold, $ V \in \xm $ and $ \omega , \eta \in \dft $. Then:
      \begin{equation}
        \ld_V (\omega \wedge \eta) = (\ld_V \omega) \wedge \eta + \omega \wedge (\ld_V \eta)
      \end{equation}
    \end{lemma}

    Since on smooth functions $ \ld_V \dd u = \dd (\ld_V u) = \dd (V(u)) $, by the inductive hypothesis and \lref{lemma:prod-rule-forms}:
    \begin{equation*}
      \ld_V (\dd u \wedge \beta) = (\ld_V \dd u) \wedge \beta + \dd u \wedge \ld_V \beta = \dd (V(u)) \wedge \beta + \dd u \wedge (\ip_V \dd \beta + \dd (\ip_V \beta))
    \end{equation*}
    On the other hand, as both $ \dd $ and $ \ip_V $ are antiderivations on $ \dft $ and $ \ip_V \dd u = \dd u(V) = V(u) $:
    \begin{equation*}
      \begin{split}
        & \ip_V \circ \dd (\dd u \wedge \beta) + \dd \circ \ip_V (\dd u \wedge \beta) \\
        & \qquad \qquad \qquad \qquad = \ip_V (- \dd u \wedge \dd \beta) + \dd (V(u) \beta - \dd u \wedge \ip_V \beta) \\
        & \qquad \qquad \qquad \qquad = - V(u) \dd \beta + \dd u \wedge \ip_V \dd \beta + \dd (V(u)) \wedge \beta + V(u) \dd \beta + \dd u \wedge \dd (\ip_V \beta)
      \end{split}
    \end{equation*}
    After cancellations, this expression is equal to the above one, proving the thesis.
  \end{proof}
\end{proofbox}

Since $ \dd^2 = 0 $, the Lie derivative commutes with the exterior derivative: $ \ld_V (\dd \omega) = \dd (\ld_V \omega) $.

\section{Integration}
\label{sec:integration}

In order to properly define integration on manifolds, it is first necessary to analyze submanifolds and to introduce the notion of orientation.

\subsection{Submanifolds}

\newpage
\subsection{Orientation}

\begin{definition}{Consistent orientation}{}
  Let $ V $ be an $ n $-dimensional $ \R $-vector space, with $ n \geq 1 $. Two ordered bases $ (E_1 , \dots , E_n) $ and $ (\tilde{E}_1 , \dots , \tilde{E}_n) $ of $ V $ are said to be \bcdef{consistently oriented} if the transition matrix $ [B_i^j] \in \R^{n \times n} : E_i = B_i^j \tilde{E}_j $ has positive determinant.
\end{definition}

Being consistently oriented is an equivalence relation on the set of all ordered bases of $ V $: it is clearly reflexive (as $ \det \mt{I}_n = n > 0 $), symmetric (as $ \det \mt{B}^{-1} = (\det \mt{B})^{-1} $) and transitive (by Binet's theorem). Moreover, the equivalence classes are only two, since the sign of the transition matrix between two bases is either positive or negative: these two equivalence classes are the two possible \bctxt{orientations} of $ V $. Given an ordered basis $ (E_1 , \dots , E_n) $ of $ V $, the orientation determined by this basis (i.e. its equivalence class) is denoted as $ [E_1 , \dots , E_n] $, while the opposite orientation as $ - [E_1 , \dots , E_n] $. In the case of $ 0 $-dimensional vector spaces, the orientation is simply defined to be a choice of one of the numbers $ \pm 1 $.

\begin{proposition}{}{ext-form-orientation}
  Let $ V $ be an $ n $-dimensional $ \R $-vector space. Each $ \omega \in \lv{n}(V) : \omega \neq 0 $ determines an orientation $ \mathcal{O}_\omega $ of $ V $ as follows:
  \begin{itemize}
    \item if $ n \geq 1 $, then $ \mathcal{O}_\omega $ is the set of ordered bases $ (E_1 , \dots , E_n) : \omega(E_1 , \dots , E_n) > 0 $;
    \item if $ n = 0 $, then $ \mathcal{O}_\omega $ is $ +1 $ if $ \omega > 0 $ and $ -1 $ if $ \omega < 0 $.
  \end{itemize}
  Two non-zero exterior $ n $-forms determine the same orientation if and only if each is a positive multiple of the other.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proofbox}
  \begin{proof}
    The $ 0 $-dimensional case is trivial, since $ \lv{0}(V) = \R $. If $ n \geq 1 $, let $ \omega \in \lv{n}(V) - \{0\} $, and let $ \mathcal{O}_\omega $ denote the set of ordered bases $ (E_1 , \dots , E_n) : \omega(E_1 , \dots , E_n) > 0 $. WTS $ \mathcal{O}_\omega $ is exactly one equivalence class.

    Suppose $ (E_i) $ and $ (\tilde{E}_i) $ are any two ordered bases of $ V $, and let $ B : V \ra V $ be a linear map sending $ E_i \mapsto \tilde{E}_i $, i.e. $ \tilde{E}_j = B^i_j E_i $. By \eref{eq:forms-determinant}:
    \begin{equation*}
      \omega(\tilde{E}_1 , \dots , \tilde{E}_n) = (\det B) \omega(E_1 , \dots , E_n)
    \end{equation*}
    Therefore, if $ (\tilde{E}_i) $ is consistently oriented with $ (E_1 , \dots , E_n) $, i.e. $ \det B > 0 $, then $ \omega(\tilde{E}_1 , \dots , \tilde{E}_n) $ and $ \omega(E_1 , \dots , E_n) $ have the same sign, i.e. $ \mathcal{O}_\omega $ is one equivalence class.
  \end{proof}
\end{proofbox}

Since $ \dim_\R \lv{n}(V) = 1 $ for any $ n $-dimensional $ \R $-vector space $ V $, choosing an orientation of $ V $ is equivalent to choosing one of the two disconnected components of $ \lv{n}(V) - \{0\} $, which are called the \emph{positive} and \emph{negative orientations}.

The concept of orientation is trivially imported on smooth manifolds in a pointwise manner: the \emph{pointwise orientation} of a smooth manifold is a choice of orientation of each tangent space. However, this concept is only useful if nearby tangent spaces have consistent orientations. Thus, if $ \man $ is a smooth $ n $-manifold, a local frame $ (E_i) $ on $ U \in \tp(T\man) $ is \bctxt{(positively) oriented} if $ (E_1 \vert_p , \dots , E_n \vert_p) $ is a positively oriented basis of $ T_p\man $ at each point $ p \in U $. Negatively oriented frames are defined analogously. A pointwise orientation is said to be \emph{continuous} if every point of $ \man $ is in the domain of an oriented local frame (since vector fields which determine a local frame are continuous by definition).

\begin{definition}{Oriented manifold}{}
  Let $ \man $ be a smooth $ n $-manifold. An \bcdef{orientation} $ \mathcal{O} $ of $ \man $ is a continuous pointwise orientation, and $ (\man,\mathcal{O}) $ is said to be an \bcdef{oriented manifold}.
\end{definition}

If such a pointwise orientation does not exist, $ \man $ is called a \emph{non-orientable manifold}. Given the chosen orientation $ \mathcal{O} $, the orientation of $ T_p\man $ is denoted by $ \mathcal{O}_p $, for each $ p \in \man $.

\begin{proposition}{Orientation and top forms}{orientation-top-forms}
  Let $ \man $ be a smooth $ n $-manifold. Any nowhere-vanishing $ n $-form $ \omega $ determines a unique orientation on $ \man $ for which $ \omega $ is positively oriented at each point. Conversely, if $ \man $ has a given orientation, then there is $ \omega \in \df{n} : \omega \neq 0 $ that is positively oriented at each point.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proofbox}
  \begin{proof}
    Let $ \omega $ be a nowhere-vanishing $ n $-form on $ \man $. By \pref{prop:ext-form-orientation}, $ \omega $ defines a pointwise orientation on $ \man $, hence only continuity needs to be checked. The case $ n = 0 $ is trivial, so assume $ n \geq 1 $. Given $ p \in \man $, let $ (E_i) $ be a local frame on a connected neighbourhood $ U $ of $ p $, and let $ (\varepsilon^i) $ be the dual coframe. Then, $ \omega = f \, \varepsilon^1 \wedge \dots \wedge \varepsilon^n $ on $ U $, for some continuous $ f : \man \ra \R $. Since $ \omega $ is nowhere-vanishing, $ f $ is nowhere-vanishing too, hence:
    \begin{equation*}
      \omega(E_1 , \dots , E_n) = f \neq 0
    \end{equation*}
    at all points of $ U $. By the connectedness of $ U $, this means that $ f $ is either always positive or always negative on $ U $, therefore the given frame is either positively or negatively oriented. If negatively, it suffices to replace $ E_1 $ with $ -E_1 $ to get a positively oriented frame. Thus, the pointwise orientation determined by $ \omega $ is continuous.

    Conversely, suppose $ \man $ is oriented, and let $ \Lambda_+^n(\man) \ssq \lm{n} $ be the open subset of positively oriented $ n $-forms at all points of $ \man $. Then, at each $ p \in \man $, the intersection of $ \Lambda_+^n(\man) $ with the fiber $ \lv{n}(T_p\man) $ is an open half-line, and therefore convex. Thus, with a suitable partition of unity, there exists a smooth global section of $ \Lambda_+^n(\man) $, i.e. a positively oriented smooth global $ n $-form.
  \end{proof}
\end{proofbox}

Because of \pref{prop:orientation-top-forms}, any nowhere-vanishing $ n $-form on $ \man $ is called an \emph{orientation form}, and if it determines the given orientation on $ \man $ it is said to be \emph{(positively) oriented}. Clearly, if $ \omega , \tilde{\omega} \in \df{n} $ are both positively oriented, then $ \tilde{\omega} = f \omega $ for some strictly positive $ f \in \cm $.

The same analysis can be made with charts. A smooth coordinate chart $ (x^i) $ on an oriented smooth manifold is said to be \emph{(positively) oriented} if the coordinate frame $ (\pa_i) $ is positively oriented, and idem for a negatively oriented one. A smooth atlas $ \{(U_\alpha,\varphi_\alpha)\} $ is said to be \emph{consistently oriented} if $ \det \jac{\varphi_\beta \circ \varphi_\alpha^{-1}} > 0 $ on $ \varphi_\alpha(U_\alpha \cap U_\beta) $, for each $ \alpha , \beta $.

\begin{proposition}{Orientation and coordiante atlases}{}
  Let $ \man $ be a smooth $ n $-manifold, with $ n \geq 1 $. Given a consistently oriented smooth atlas of $ \man $, there is a unique orientation of $ \man $ such that each chart in the atlas is positively oriented. Conversely, if $ \man $ is oriented and either $ \pa \man = \emptyset $ or $ n > 1 $, then the collection of all oriented smooth charts is a consistently oriented atlas of $ \man $.
\end{proposition}

\section{Connections}

There is a different way to differentiate tensor fields, distinct from the Lie derivative, associated to a different way to map different vector spaces at different points: the covariant derivative.

For the rest of this chapter, $ \mathcal{M} $ is implied to be an $ n $-dimensional metric manifold with metric tensor $ g $.

\subsection{Covariant derivative}

\begin{definition}{Connection}{}
  The \bcdef{connection} is a map $ \na : \xm \times \xm \rightarrow \xm $, usually written as $ \na(X,Y) \equiv \na_X Y $, where $ \na_X $ is called the \bcdef{covariant derivative}, satisfying the following properties for all $ X,Y,Z \in \xm $ and $ f,g \in \cm $:
  \begin{enumerate}
    \item $ \na_X (Y + Z) = \na_X Y + \na_X Z $;
    \item $ \na_{fX + gY} Z = f \na_X Z + g \na_Y Z $;
    \item $ \na_X (fY) = f \na_X Y + X(f) Y $.
  \end{enumerate}
\end{definition}

Usually $ X(f) \equiv \na_X f $. The covariant derivative endows the manifold with more structure: in particular, given a basis $ \{e_{\mu}\}_{\mu = 1 , \dots , n} $ of $ \xm $, its covariant derivative is expressed as:
\begin{equation}
  \na_{e_{\rho}} e_{\nu} \equiv \Gamma^{\mu}_{\rho \nu} e_{\mu}
\end{equation}
The $ \Gamma^{\mu}_{\rho \nu} $ are the components of the connection on that basis. Conventionally $ \na_{e_{\mu}} \equiv \na_{\mu} $, thus resembling a partial derivative. To elucidate how the covariant derivative acts on vector fields:
\begin{equation*}
  \begin{split}
    \na_X Y
    &= \na_X (Y^\mu e_\mu) \\
    &= X(Y^\mu) e_\mu + Y^\mu \na_X e_\mu \\
    &= X^{\nu} e_{\nu} (Y^{\mu}) e_{\mu} + Y^{\mu} X^{\nu} \na_{\nu} e_{\mu} \\
    &= X^{\nu} \left[ e_{\nu}(Y^{\mu}) + \Gamma^{\mu}_{\nu \rho} Y^{\rho} \right] e_{\mu} \\
    &= X^{\nu} \na_{\nu} Y = X^{\nu} (\na_{\nu} Y)^{\mu} e_{\mu}
  \end{split}
\end{equation*}
The dependency on $ X $ can therefore be eliminated, and in components:
\begin{equation}
  (\na_\nu Y)^\mu = e_\nu (Y^\mu) + \Gamma^\mu_{\nu \rho} Y^\rho
\end{equation}
A sloppy notation is often used: $ (\na_\nu Y)^\mu \equiv \na_\nu Y^\mu $. This must not be confused as the covariant derivative of $ Y^\mu $. Moreover $ \na_\nu Y^\mu \equiv \tensor{Y}{^\mu_{;\nu}} $, while $ \pa_\mu f \equiv f_{,\mu} $. On the coordinate basis $ e_\mu = \pa_\mu $, then:
\begin{equation}
  \tensor{Y}{^\mu_{;\nu}} = \tensor{Y}{^\mu_{,\nu}} + \Gamma^\mu_{\nu \rho} Y^\rho
  \label{eq:covariant-derivative-vector}
\end{equation}
Note that $ \tensor{Y}{^\mu_{;\nu}} $ is the $ \mu^{\text{th}} $ component of $ \na_\nu Y $, while $ \tensor{Y}{^\mu_{,\nu}} $ is the partial derivative of $ Y^{\mu} $ along $ \pa_{\nu} $.

The covariant derivative coincides with other derivatives on $ \cm $: it can be shown that $ \na_X f = \mathcal{L}_X f = X(f) $ and $ \na_\mu f = \pa_\mu f $. On $ \xm $, however, $ \na_X $ and $ \mathcal{L}_X $ are distinct: while $ \na_X = X^\mu \na_\mu $, there is no way to write the same relation for $ \mathcal{L}_X $, for it depends not only on $ X $ but on its first derivative too. The covariant derivative is thus the natural generalization of the partial derivative to curved manifolds.

\begin{proposition}{}{gamma-non-tens}
  $ \Gamma^\mu_{\rho \nu} $ are not components of a tensor.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proofbox}
  \begin{proof}
    Given the basis transformation $ \tilde{e}_\nu = \tensor{A}{^\mu _\nu} e_\mu $, with $ A $ and invertible matrix (if they are both coordinate basis, then $ \tensor{A}{^\mu _\nu} = \frac{\pa x^\mu}{\pa y^\nu} $), the components of a (1,2) tensor must transform as:
    \begin{equation*}
      \tensor{\tilde{T}}{^\mu _\rho _\nu} = \tensor{(A^{-1})}{^\mu _\tau} \tensor{A}{^\sigma _\rho} \tensor{A}{^\lambda _\nu} \tensor{T}{^\tau _\sigma _\lambda}
    \end{equation*}
    In the new basis:
    \begin{equation*}
      \begin{split}
        \tilde{\Gamma}^\mu_{\rho \nu} \tilde{e}_\mu
        &= \na_{\tilde{e}_\rho}\tilde{e}_\nu = \na_{\tensor{A}{^\sigma _\rho} e_\sigma} (\tensor{A}{^\lambda _\nu} e_{\nu}) = \tensor{A}{^\sigma _\rho} \na_{e_\sigma} (\tensor{A}{^\lambda _\nu} e_\lambda) \\
        &= \tensor{A}{^\sigma _\rho} \tensor{A}{^\lambda _\nu} \Gamma^\tau_{\sigma \lambda} e_\tau + \tensor{A}{^\sigma _\rho} e_\lambda \pa_\sigma \tensor{A}{^\lambda _\nu} = \left[ \tensor{A}{^\sigma _\rho} \tensor{A}{^\lambda _\nu} \Gamma^\tau_{\sigma \lambda} + \tensor{A}{^\sigma _\rho} \pa_\sigma \tensor{A}{^\tau _\nu} \right] e_\tau \\
        &= \left[ \tensor{A}{^\sigma _\rho} \tensor{A}{^\lambda _\nu} \Gamma^\tau_{\sigma \lambda} + \tensor{A}{^\sigma _\rho} \pa_\sigma \tensor{A}{^\tau _\nu} \right] \tensor{(A^{-1})}{^\mu _\tau} \tilde{e}_\mu
      \end{split}
    \end{equation*}
    Thus, there is a second term proportional to $ \pa A $ which deviates from the transformation law:
    \begin{equation*}
      \tilde{\Gamma}^\mu_{\rho \nu} = \tensor{(A^{-1})}{^\mu _\tau} \tensor{A}{^\sigma _\rho} \left[ \tensor{A}{^\lambda _\nu} \Gamma^\tau_{\sigma \lambda} + \pa_\sigma \tensor{A}{^\tau _\nu} \right]
    \end{equation*}
    which means that $ \Gamma^\mu_{\rho \nu} $ is not a tensor.
  \end{proof}
\end{proofbox}

\subsection{Covariant derivative of tensors}

First of all, it is necessary to elucidate how the covariant derivative acts on 1-forms. Given a 1-form $ \omega $, the 1-form $ \na_X \omega $ is defined by its action on vector fields. By Leibniz rule:
\begin{equation*}
  \na_X (\omega(Y)) = (\na_X \omega)(Y) + \omega(\na_X Y)
\end{equation*}
Recalling that $ \omega(Y) $ is a function, $ \na_X (\omega(Y)) = X(\omega(Y)) $, therefore:
\begin{equation}
  (\na_X \omega)(Y) = X(\omega(Y)) - \omega(\na_X Y)
  \label{eq:cov-der-form-aux}
\end{equation}
Expressing it in coordinates:
\begin{equation*}
  \begin{split}
    X^\mu (\na_\mu \omega)_\nu Y^\nu
    &= X^\mu \pa_\mu (\omega_\nu Y^\nu) - \omega_\nu X^\mu \left[ \pa_\mu Y^\nu + \Gamma^\nu_{\mu \rho} Y^\rho \right] \\
    &= X^\mu \left[ \pa_\mu \omega_\rho - \Gamma^\nu_{\mu \rho} \omega_{\nu} \right] Y^\rho
  \end{split}
\end{equation*}
Crucially, the $ \pa Y $ terms cancel out, allowing to define $ \na_X \omega $ without referencing $ Y $:
\begin{equation}
  (\na_\mu \omega)_\rho = \pa_\mu \omega_\rho - \Gamma^\nu_{\mu \rho} \omega_\nu
\end{equation}
Using the same notation as for vector fields $ (\na_\mu \omega)_\rho \equiv \na_\mu \omega_\rho \equiv \omega_{\rho ; \mu} $:
\begin{equation}
  \omega_{\rho ;\mu} = \omega_{\rho,\mu} - \Gamma^\nu_{\mu \rho} \omega_\nu
\end{equation}
This kind of argument can be extended to a general $ (p,q) $ tensor field:
\begin{equation}
  \begin{split}
    \na_\rho \tensor{T}{^{\mu_1} ^\dots ^{\mu_p} _{\nu_1} _\dots _{\mu_q}} = \pa_\rho \tensor{T}{^{\mu_1} ^\dots ^{\mu_p} _{\nu_1} _\dots _{\nu_q}}
    &+ \Gamma^{\mu_1}_{\rho \sigma} \tensor{T}{^\sigma ^{\mu_2} ^\dots ^{\mu_p} _{\nu_1} _\dots _{\nu_q}} + \dots + \Gamma^{\mu_p}_{\rho \sigma} \tensor{T}{^{\mu_1} ^\dots ^{\mu_{p-1}} ^\sigma _{\nu_1} _\dots _{\nu_q}} \\
    &- \Gamma^\sigma_{\rho \nu_1} \tensor{T}{^{\mu_1} ^\dots ^{\mu_p} _\sigma _{\nu_2} _\dots _{\nu_q}} - \dots - \Gamma^\sigma_{\rho \nu_q} \tensor{T}{^{\mu_1} ^\dots ^{\mu_p} _{\nu_1} _\dots _{\nu_{q-1}} _\sigma}
  \end{split}
\end{equation}
The pattern is clear: for each upper index $ \mu $ there is a $ + \Gamma^\mu_{\rho \sigma} T^\sigma $ term, while for each lower index $ \nu $ there is $ - \Gamma^\sigma_{\rho \nu} T_\sigma $ term. Furthermore, it is necessary to generalize the comma-notation: for example, $ \tensor{X}{^\mu _; _\nu _\rho} \equiv \na_\rho \na_\nu X^\mu $, so the rightmost index is the one acting first.

\subsubsection{Torsion and curvature}

Even though the connection is not a tensor, it is used to construct two important tensors.

\begin{definition}{Torsion tensor}{}
  The \bcdef{torsion} is a (1,2) tensor defined on $ \lm{1} \times \xm \times \xm $ as:
  \begin{equation}
    \tors(\omega,X,Y) \defeq \omega (\na_X Y - \na_Y X - [X,Y])
  \end{equation}
  Alternatively, the torsion can be viewed as a map $ \tors : \xm \times \xm \rightarrow \xm $ such that:
  \begin{equation}
    \tors(X,Y) = \na_X Y - \na_Y X - [X,Y]
  \end{equation}
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}{Curvature tensor}{}
  The \bcdef{curvature} is a (1,3) tensor defined on $ \lm{1} \times \xm \times \xm \times \xm $ as:
  \begin{equation}
    \riem(\omega,Z,X,Y) \defeq \omega (\na_X \na_Y Z - \na_Y \na_X Z - \na_{[X,Y]} Z)
    \label{eq:riemann-def}
  \end{equation}
  Alternatively, the curvature can be viewed as a map from $ \xm \times \xm $ to the space of differential operators on $ \xm $ such that:
  \begin{equation}
    \riem(X,Y) = \na_X \na_Y - \na_Y \na_X - \na_{[X,Y]}
    \label{eq:riemann-operator-def}
  \end{equation}
\end{definition}

The fact that these are indeed tensors, i.e. they are linear in each argument, can be shown by direct calculation, recalling that $ [fX,Y] = f[X,Y] - Y(f) X $.

\begin{proposition}{}{}
  On the coordinate basis $ \{\pa_\mu\} $ and $ \{\dd x^\mu\} $ the torsion components are:
  \begin{equation}
    \tensor{T}{^\rho _\mu _\nu} = \Gamma^\rho_{\mu \nu} - \Gamma^\rho_{\nu \mu}
    \label{eq:torsion-comp}
  \end{equation}
\end{proposition}

\begin{proofbox}
  \begin{proof}
    By direct calculation:
    \begin{equation*}
      \begin{split}
        \tensor{T}{^\rho _\mu _\nu}
        &= T(\dd x^\rho, \pa_\mu, \pa_\nu) = \dd x^\mu (\na_\mu \pa_\nu - \na_\nu \pa_\mu - [\pa_\mu, \pa_\nu]) \\
        &= \dd x^\mu (\pa_\mu \pa_\nu - \Gamma^\sigma_{\mu \nu} \pa_\sigma - \pa_\nu \pa_\mu + \Gamma^\sigma_{\nu \mu} \pa_\sigma) \\
        &= \left[ \Gamma^\sigma_{\mu \nu} - \Gamma^\sigma_{\nu \mu} \right] \delta^\mu_\sigma = \Gamma^\rho_{\mu \nu} - \Gamma^\rho_{\nu \mu}
      \end{split}
    \end{equation*}
    which is the thesis.
  \end{proof}
\end{proofbox}

Interestingly, even though $ \Gamma^\rho_{\mu \nu} $ is not a tensor, its anti-symmetric part $ \Gamma^\rho_{[\mu \nu]} = \frac{1}{2} \tensor{T}{^\sigma_\mu _\nu} $ is. Clearly, the torsion tensor is anti-symmetric in its lower indices, thus for connections which are symmetric in their lower indices the torsion is null: such connections are said to be \bctxt{torsion-free}.

\begin{proposition}{}{}
  On the coordinate basis $ \{\pa_\mu\} $ and $ \{\dd x^\mu\} $ the curvature components are:
  \begin{equation}
    \tensor{R}{^\sigma _\rho _\mu _\nu} = \pa_\mu \Gamma^\sigma_{\nu \rho} - \pa_\nu \Gamma^\sigma_{\mu \rho} + \Gamma^\lambda_{\nu \rho} \Gamma^\sigma_{\mu \lambda} - \Gamma^\lambda_{\mu \rho} \Gamma^\sigma_{\nu \lambda}
    \label{eq:riemann-comp}
  \end{equation}
\end{proposition}

\begin{proofbox}
  \begin{proof}
    By direct calculation:
    \begin{equation*}
      \begin{split}
        \riem(\dd x^\sigma, \pa_\rho, \pa_\mu, \pa_\nu)
        &= \dd x^\sigma (\na_\mu \na_\nu \pa_\rho - \na_\nu \na_\mu \pa_\rho - \na_{[\pa_\mu, \pa_\nu]} \pa_\rho) \\
        &= \dd x^\sigma (\na_\mu \na_\nu \pa_\rho - \na_\nu \na_\mu \pa_\rho) \\
        &= \dd x^\sigma (\na_\mu (\Gamma^\lambda_{\nu \rho} \pa_\lambda) - \na_\nu (\Gamma^\lambda_{\mu \rho} \pa_\lambda)) \\
        &= \dd x^\sigma ((\pa_\mu \Gamma^\lambda_{\nu \rho}) \pa_\lambda + \Gamma^\lambda_{\nu \rho} \Gamma^\tau_{\mu \lambda} \pa_\tau - (\pa_\nu \Gamma^\lambda_{\mu \rho}) \pa_\lambda - \Gamma^\lambda_{\mu \rho} \Gamma^\tau_{\nu \lambda} \pa_{\tau}) \\
        &= \pa_\mu \Gamma^\sigma_{\nu \rho} - \pa_\nu \Gamma^\sigma_{\mu \rho} + \Gamma^\lambda_{\nu \rho} \Gamma^\sigma_{\mu \lambda} - \Gamma^\lambda_{\mu \rho} \Gamma^\sigma_{\nu \lambda}
      \end{split}
    \end{equation*}
    which completes the proof.
  \end{proof}
\end{proofbox}

Clearly, the curvature tensor is anti-symmetric in its last two lower indices, i.e. $ \tensor{R}{^\sigma _\rho _\mu _\nu} = \tensor{R}{^\sigma _\rho _[ _\mu _\nu _]} $, and it can in fact be written as:
\begin{equation}
  \tensor{R}{^\sigma _\rho _\mu _\nu} = 2 \pa_{[\mu} \Gamma^\sigma_{\nu] \rho} + 2 \Gamma^\sigma_{[\mu | \lambda |} \Gamma^\lambda_{\nu]\rho}
\end{equation}

\begin{theorem}[before upper = {\tcbtitle}]{Ricci identity}{}
  \begin{equation}
    2 \na_{[\mu} \na_{\nu]} Z^\sigma = \tensor{R}{^\sigma _\rho _\mu _\nu} Z^\rho - \tensor{T}{^\rho_{\mu \nu}} \na_\rho Z^\sigma
    \label{eq:ricci-id}
  \end{equation}
\end{theorem}

\begin{proofbox}
  \begin{proof}
    By direct calculation:
    \begin{equation*}
      \begin{split}
        \na_{[\mu} \na_{\nu]} Z^\sigma
        &= \pa_{[\mu} (\na_{\nu]} Z^\sigma) + \Gamma^\sigma_{[\mu | \lambda |} \na_{\nu]} Z^\lambda - \Gamma^\rho_{[\mu \nu]} \na_\rho Z^\sigma \\
        &= \pa_{[\mu} \pa_{\nu]} Z^\sigma + (\pa_{[\mu} \Gamma^\sigma_{\nu]\rho}) Z^\rho + (\pa_{[\mu} Z^\rho) \Gamma^\sigma_{\nu]\rho} + \Gamma^\sigma_{[\mu | \lambda |} \pa_{\nu]} Z\lambda + \Gamma^\sigma_{[\mu | \lambda |} \Gamma^\lambda_{\nu] \rho} Z^\rho - \frac{1}{2} \tensor{T}{^\rho_{\mu \nu}} \na_\rho Z^\sigma \\
        &= \left( \pa_{[\mu} \Gamma^\sigma_{\nu]\rho} + \Gamma^\sigma_{[\mu | \lambda |} \Gamma^\lambda_{\nu]\rho} \right) Z^\rho - \frac{1}{2} \tensor{T}{^\rho_{\mu \nu}} \na_\rho Z^\sigma \\
        &= \frac{1}{2} \tensor{R}{^\sigma_{\rho \mu \nu}} Z^\rho - \frac{1}{2} \tensor{T}{^\sigma_{\mu \nu}} \na_\rho Z^\sigma
      \end{split}
    \end{equation*}
    which is the thesis.
  \end{proof}
\end{proofbox}

\subsubsection{Levi--Civita connection}

The discussion on the connection has so far been independent of the metric. Starting to consider it, an important result is the fundamental theorem of Riemannian geometry.

\begin{theorem}{Riemann's theorem}{}
  On a metric manifold $ (\mathcal{M},g) $, there exists a unique torsion-free connection that is compatible with the metric, i.e. for all $ X \in \xm $:
  \begin{equation}
    \na_X g = 0
  \end{equation}
  This is called the \bcth{Levi--Civita connection}.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proofbox}
  \begin{proof}
    WTS uniqueness: suppose such a connection exists. Then, by Leibniz rule:
    \begin{equation*}
      X(g(Y,Z)) = \na_X (g(Y,Z)) = (\na_X g)(Y,Z) + g(\na_X Y, Z) + g(Y, \na_X, Z)
    \end{equation*}
    Since $ \na_X g = 0 $, by cyclic permutations of $ X $, $ Y $ and $ Z $:
    \begin{equation*}
      \begin{split}
        X(g(Y,Z)) &= g(\na_X Y, Z) + g(Y, \na_X Z) \\
        Y(g(Z,X)) &= g(\na_Y Z, X) + g(Z, \na_Y X) \\
        Z(g(X,Y)) &= g(\na_Z X, Y) + g(X, \na_Z Y)
      \end{split}
    \end{equation*}
    Since the connection is torsion-free, $ \na_X Y - \na_Y X = [X,Y] $, thus these equations become:
    \begin{equation*}
      \begin{split}
        X(g(Y,Z)) &= g(\na_Y X, Z) + g(\na_X Z, Y) + g([X,Y], Z) \\
        Y(g(Z,X)) &= g(\na_Z Y, X) + g(\na_Y X, Z) + g([Y,Z], X) \\
        Z(g(X,Y)) &= g(\na_X Z, Y) + g(\na_Z Y, X) + g([Z,X], y)
      \end{split}
    \end{equation*}
    Adding the first two and subtracting the third:
    \begin{equation*}
      \begin{split}
        g(\na_Y X, Z) = \frac{1}{2} [ & X(g(Y,Z)) + Y(g(Z,X)) + Z(g(X,Y)) \\
                                      & - g([X,Y],Z) - g([Y,Z],X) + g([Z,X],Y) ]
      \end{split}
    \end{equation*}
    The metric is non-degenerate, thus this uniquely specifies the connection. By direct calculation it can be shown that it indeed satisfies all the properties of a connection.
  \end{proof}
\end{proofbox}

It is possible to explicit the components of the Levi--Civita connection in terms of the metric.

\begin{proposition}{Christoffel symbols}{}
  On the coordinate basis $ \{\pa_\mu\} $ and $ \{dx^\mu\} $ the Levi--Civita connection's components, called \bcprop{Christoffel symbols}, are:
  \begin{equation}
    \Gamma^\lambda_{\mu \nu} = \frac{1}{2} g^{\lambda \rho} (\pa_\mu g_{\nu \rho} + \pa_\nu g_{\mu \rho} - \pa_\rho g_{\mu \nu})
  \end{equation}
\end{proposition}

\begin{proofbox}
  \begin{proof}
    Recalling that $ [\pa_\mu, \pa_\nu] = 0 $, $ \Gamma^\lambda_{\mu \nu} g_{\lambda \rho} = g(\na_\mu \pa_\nu, \pa_\rho) = \frac{1}{2} (\pa_\nu g_{\mu \rho} + \pa_\mu g_{\nu \rho} - \pa_\rho g_{\mu \nu}) $.
  \end{proof}
\end{proofbox}

\begin{example}{Euclidean space}{}
  In flat space $ \R^n $, endowed with either Euclidean or Minkowski metric, it is always possible to choose Cartesian coordinates, in which case the Christoffel symbols vanish. Being the Riemann tensor a genuine tensor, it therefore will vanish in all possible coordinate systems on $ \R^n $, even in those with $ \Gamma^\rho_{\mu \nu} \neq 0 $: this expresses the flatness of $ \R^n $.
\end{example}

\subsubsection{Einstein tensor}

In addition to the anti-symmetry in its last two indices, the Riemann tensor presents additional subtle symmetries when using the Levi--Civita connection.

\begin{proposition}{Symmetries of the Riemann tensor}{riemann-symmetries}
  On a metric manifold with a Levi--Civita connection:
  \begin{equation}
    R_{\sigma \rho \mu \nu} = - R_{\sigma \rho \nu \mu} = - R_{\rho \sigma \mu \nu} = R_{\mu \nu \sigma \rho}
    \qquad \qquad
    R_{\sigma [\rho \mu \nu]} = 0
    \label{eq:riemann-symm}
  \end{equation}
\end{proposition}

\begin{proofbox}
  \begin{proof}
    Set normal coordinates centered at a point $ p $: then, $ \Gamma^\mu_{\nu \rho} = 0 $ and $ \pa_\mu g^{\lambda \sigma} = 0 $ at that point. At $ p $, the Riemann tensor can be written as:
    \begin{equation*}
      \begin{split}
        R_{\sigma \rho \mu \nu}
        &= g_{\sigma \lambda} \tensor{R}{^\lambda _{\rho \mu \nu}} = g_{\sigma \lambda} \left[ \pa_\mu \Gamma^\lambda_{\nu \rho} - \pa_\nu \Gamma^\lambda_{\mu \rho} \right] \\
        &= \frac{1}{2} \left[ \pa_\mu \left( \pa_\nu g_{\sigma \rho} + \pa_\rho g_{\nu \sigma} - \pa_\sigma g_{\nu \rho} \right) - \pa_\nu \left( \pa_\mu g_{\sigma \rho} + \pa_\rho g_{\mu \sigma} - \pa_\sigma g_{\mu \rho} \right) \right] \\
        &= \frac{1}{2} \left[ \pa_\mu \pa_\rho g_{\nu \sigma} - \pa_\mu \pa_\sigma g_{\nu \rho} - \pa_\nu \pa_\rho g_{\mu \sigma} + \pa_\nu \pa_\sigma g_{\mu \rho} \right]
      \end{split}
    \end{equation*}
    The symmetries are then manifest, and being these tensor equations they are valid in all coordinate systems.
  \end{proof}
\end{proofbox}

An important computation tool is the Bianchi identity.

\begin{theorem}{Bianchi identity}{}
  On a metric manifold with a Levi--Civita connection:
  \begin{equation}
    \na_{[\lambda} R_{\sigma \rho] \mu \nu} = 0
    \quad \iff \quad
    \tensor{R}{^\sigma_{\rho [\mu \nu ; \lambda]}} = 0
    \label{eq:riemann-bianchi}
  \end{equation}
\end{theorem}

\begin{proofbox}
  \begin{proof}
    The two equations are equivalent, so the proof is of the first one. In normal coordinates $ \na_\mu = \pa_\mu $ at $ p $, so schematically: $ R = \pa \Gamma + \Gamma \Gamma $, thus $ \na R = \pa R = \pa^2 \Gamma + \Gamma \pa \Gamma = \pa^2 \Gamma $. Explicitly:
    \begin{equation*}
      \pa_\lambda R_{\sigma \rho \mu \nu} = \frac{1}{2} \pa_\lambda \left[ \pa_\mu \pa_\rho g_{\nu \sigma} - \pa_\mu \pa_\sigma g_{\nu \rho} - \pa_\nu \pa_\rho g_{\mu \sigma} + \pa_\nu \pa_\sigma g_{\mu \rho} \right]
    \end{equation*}
    Anti-symmetrizing the appropriate indices yields the result.
  \end{proof}
\end{proofbox}

Note that \eeref{eq:riemann-symm}{eq:riemann-bianchi} do not require that the connection is a Levi--Civita connection, but are valid for general torsion-free connections.

\begin{definition}{Ricci tensor and scalar}{}
  On a metric manifold, the \bcdef{Ricci tensor} is defined as:
  \begin{equation}
    R_{\mu \nu} \defeq \tensor{R}{^\rho _{\mu \rho \nu}}
  \end{equation}
  The \bcdef{Ricci scalar} is defined as:
  \begin{equation}
    R \defeq g^{\mu \nu} R_{\mu \nu}
  \end{equation}
\end{definition}

From \eref{eq:riemann-symm}, the Ricci tensor is clearly symmetric: $ R_{\mu \nu} = R_{\nu \mu} $.

\begin{proposition}{}{}
  On a metric manifold:
  \begin{equation}
    \na^\mu R_{\mu \nu} = \frac{1}{2} \na_\nu R
    \label{eq:ricci}
  \end{equation}
\end{proposition}

\begin{proofbox}
  \begin{proof}
    Writing explicitly Bianchi identity:
    \begin{equation*}
      \na_\lambda R_{\sigma \rho \mu \nu} + \na_\sigma R_{\rho \lambda \mu \nu} + \na_\rho R_{\lambda \sigma \mu \nu} = 0
    \end{equation*}
    Contracting with $ g^{\mu \lambda} g^{\rho \nu} $:
    \begin{equation*}
      \na^\mu R_{\mu \sigma} - \na_\sigma R + \na^\nu R_{\nu \sigma} = 0
    \end{equation*}
    which yields the thesis.
  \end{proof}
\end{proofbox}

An important tensor obtained as a combination of the Ricci tensor and scalar is the \bctxt{Einstein tensor}, which encodes information on the curvature of the metric manifold on which it is defined:
\begin{equation}
  G_{\mu \nu} \defeq R_{\mu \nu} - \frac{1}{2} R g_{\mu \nu}
  \label{eq:einstein-def}
\end{equation}
Most importantly, the Einstein tensor is covariantly conserved, as of \eref{eq:ricci}:
\begin{equation}
  \na^\mu G_{\mu \nu} = 0
\end{equation}

\subsubsection{Gauss' theorem}

The divergence theorem (or Gauss' theorem) states that the integral of a total derivative is a boundary term. It is possible to express this theorem on curved manifolds in a convenient way.

\begin{lemma}[before upper = {\tcbtitle}]{}{gauss-lemma}
  \begin{equation}
    \Gamma^\mu_{\mu \nu} = \frac{1}{\sqg} \pa_\nu \sqg
  \end{equation}
\end{lemma}

\begin{proofbox}
  \begin{proof}
    A useful identity for invertible matrices: $ \tr \log \mt{A} = \log \det \mt{A} $. Thus (WLOG $ \det g > 0 $):
    \begin{equation*}
      \begin{split}
        \Gamma^\mu_{\mu \nu}
        &= \frac{1}{2} g^{\mu \rho} \pa_\nu g_{\mu \rho} = \frac{1}{2} \tr (g^{-1} \pa_\nu g) = \frac{1}{2} \tr (\pa_\nu \log g) = \frac{1}{2} \pa_\nu \log \det g = \frac{1}{\sqrt{\det g}} \pa_\nu \sqrt{\det g}
      \end{split}
    \end{equation*}
    which is the thesis.
  \end{proof}
\end{proofbox}

\begin{theorem}{Gauss' theorem}{}
  Given a Riemannian manifold $ (\mathcal{M},g) $, consider a region $ M \subseteq \mathcal{M} $ with boundary $ \pa M $ and let $ n^\mu $ be an outward-pointing unit vector orthogonal to $ \pa M $. Then, for any vector field $ X^\mu $ on $ M $:
  \begin{equation}
    \int_M \dd^n x \sqg\, \na_\mu X^\mu = \int_{\pa M} \dd^{n-1} x \sqrt{\gamma}\, n_\mu X^\mu
    \label{eq:gauss-theorem}
  \end{equation}
  where $ \gamma_{ij} $ is the pull-back of the metric to $ \pa M $ and $ \gamma \equiv \det \gamma_{ij} $.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proofbox}
  \begin{proof}
    From \lref{lemma:gauss-lemma}:
    \begin{equation*}
      \sqg\, \na_\mu X^\mu = \sqg \left( \pa_\mu X^\mu + \Gamma^\mu_{\mu \nu} X^\nu \right) = \sqg \left( \pa_\mu X^\mu + X^\nu \frac{1}{\sqg} \pa_\nu \sqg \right) = \pa_\mu ( \sqg X^\mu )
    \end{equation*}
    The integral becomes:
    \begin{equation*}
      \int_M \dd^n x \sqg\, \na_\mu X^\mu = \int_M \dd^n x\, \pa_\mu (\sqg X^\mu)
    \end{equation*}
    This is the integral of an ordinary partial derivative, so the ordinary divergence theorem applies. To evaluate the integral on the boundary, it is convenient to pick coordinates so that $ \pa M $ is a surface at constant $ x^n $. Moreover, to simplify the proof, the possible metrics will be restricted to $ g_{\mu \nu} = \diag \left( \gamma_{ij}, N^2 \right) $. By usual integration rules:
    \begin{equation*}
      \int_M \dd^n x\, \pa_\mu ( \sqg X^\mu) = \int_{\pa M} \dd^{n-1} x \sqrt{\gamma N^2} X^n
    \end{equation*}
    The unit normal vector is $ n^\mu = (0,\dots,0,\frac{1}{N}) $, so that $ g_{\mu \nu} n^\mu n^\nu = 1 $, therefore $ n_\mu = g_{\mu \nu} n^\nu = (0,\dots,0,N) $. The proof is then concluded because:
    \begin{equation*}
      \int_{\pa M} \dd^{n-1} x\sqrt{\gamma N^2} X^n = \int_{\pa M} \dd^{n-1} x \sqrt{\gamma} n_\mu X^\mu
    \end{equation*}
    which is the thesis.
  \end{proof}
\end{proofbox}

Note that this theorem holds on Lorentzian manifolds too, with the condition that $ \pa M $ must be purely timelike or purely spacelike, ensuring that $ \gamma \neq 0 $ at any point.

\section{Parallel transport}

The connection connects tangent spaces, or more generally any tensor vector space, at different points of the manifold: this map is called parallel transport and it's necessary for the definition of differentiation.

\begin{definition}{Parallel transport}{}
  Consider a vector field $ X $ and some associated integral curve $ \gamma $, with coordinates $ x^\mu(\tau) $ such that:
  \begin{equation*}
    X^\mu \big\vert_\gamma = \frac{\dd x^\mu(\tau)}{\dd \tau}
  \end{equation*}
  A tensor field $ T $ is said to be \bcdef{parallely transported} along $ \gamma $ if:
  \begin{equation}
    \na_X T = 0
    \label{eq:parallel-transport}
  \end{equation}
\end{definition}

Suppose that $ \gamma $ connects two points $ p,q \in \mathcal{M} $: \eref{eq:parallel-transport} provides a map from the tensor vector space defined at $ p $ to that defined at $ q $. To illustrate this, consider the parallel transport of a vector field $ Y $:
\begin{equation*}
  X^\nu (\pa_\nu Y^\mu + \Gamma^\mu_{\nu \rho} Y^\rho) = 0
\end{equation*}
Evaluating this equation on $ \gamma $, considering $ Y^\mu = Y^\mu(x(\tau)) $:
\begin{equation}
  \frac{\dd Y^\mu}{\dd \tau} + X^\nu \Gamma^\mu_{\nu \rho} Y^\rho = 0
\end{equation}
These are a set of coupled ODEs, thus, given an initial condition (e.g. at $ \tau = 0 $, i.e. at $ p $), these equations can be solved to find a unique vector at each point along the curve.

Note that the parallel transport depends both on the path (characterized by the vector field $ X $) and on the connection.

\begin{definition}{Geodesic}{}
  Given a vector field $ X $, a \bcdef{geodesic} is an integral curve to $ X $ such that:
  \begin{equation}
    \na_X X = 0
    \label{eq:geodesic-def}
  \end{equation}
\end{definition}

\begin{proposition}{Geodesic equation}{}
  A geodesic is described by:
  \begin{equation}
    \frac{\dd^2 x^\mu}{\dd \tau^2} + \Gamma^\mu_{\nu \rho} \frac{\dd x^\nu}{\dd \tau} \frac{\dd x^\rho}{\dd \tau} = 0
    \label{eq:geodesic-equation}
  \end{equation}
\end{proposition}

\begin{proofbox}
  \begin{proof}
    From the above calculations, along $ \gamma $:
    \begin{equation*}
      0 = \frac{\dd X^\mu}{\dd \tau} + \Gamma^\mu_{\nu \rho} X^\nu X^\rho = \frac{\dd^2 x^\mu}{\dd \tau^2} + \Gamma^\mu_{\nu \rho} \frac{\dd x^\nu}{\dd \tau} \frac{\dd x^\rho}{\dd \tau}
    \end{equation*}
    which is the geodesic equation.
  \end{proof}
\end{proofbox}

For the Levi--Civita connection $ \na_X g = 0 $. If $ Y \in \xm $ is parallely transported along a geodesic associated to $ X \in \xm $, then $ \na_X Y = \na_X X = 0 $, therefore $ \frac{\dd}{\dd \tau} g(X,Y) = 0 $: this ensures that the two tangent vectors always determine the same angles along the geodesic.

\subsection{Normal coordinates}

\begin{theorem}{Equivalence principle}{equivalence}
  Given a Riemannian manifold $ (\mathcal{M},g) $ and $ p \in \mathcal{M} $, in a neighbourhood of $ p $ it's always possible to find coordinates, called \bcth{normal coordinates}, such that $ g_{\mu \nu}(p) = \delta_{\mu \nu} $ and $ g_{\mu \nu , \rho}(p) = 0 $.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proofbox}
  \begin{proof}
    By brute force, consider initial coordinates $ y^\mu $ and find a change of coordinates $ x^\mu $ which satisfy the requirements:
    \begin{equation*}
      \frac{\pa y^\rho}{\pa x^\mu} \frac{\pa y^\sigma}{\pa x^\nu} \tilde{g}_{\rho \sigma} = g_{\mu \nu}
    \end{equation*}
    WLOG $ p $ is the origin of both sets of coordinates, so:
    \begin{equation*}
      y^\rho = \frac{\pa y^\rho}{\pa x^\mu}\bigg\vert_{x = 0} x^\mu + \frac{1}{2} \frac{\pa^2 y^\rho}{\pa x^\mu \pa x^\nu}\bigg\vert_{x = 0} x^\mu x^\nu + \dots
    \end{equation*}
    This, together with the Taylor expansion of $ \tilde{g}_{\rho \sigma} $, can be inserted in the transformation equation of the metric, thus finding a set of PDEs for each power of $ x $, which can be solved to characterize the normal coordinates. For example, the first condition is:
    \begin{equation*}
      \frac{\pa y^\rho}{\pa x^\mu}\bigg\vert_{x = 0} \frac{\pa y^\sigma}{\pa x^\nu}\bigg\vert_{x = 0} \tilde{g}_{\rho \sigma}(p) = \delta_{\mu \nu}
    \end{equation*}
    Given any $ \tilde{g}_{\rho \sigma}(p) $, it's always possible to find $ \frac{\pa y}{\pa x} $ that satisfies this condition. In fact, if $ \dim_{\R} \mathcal{M} = n $, the Jacobian of the transformation has $ n^2 $ independent elements and the equation above puts $ \frac{1}{2} n (n + 1) $ constraints: the remaining free parameters are $ \frac{1}{2} n (n - 1) $, which is precisely the dimension of $ \SOn{n} $, the symmetry group of the flat metric.
    A similar counting shows that $ g_{\mu \nu , \rho}(p) = 0 $ puts $ \frac{1}{2} n^2 (n + 1) $ constraints, precisely the number of independent elements of the Hessian of the transformation.
  \end{proof}
\end{proofbox}

This theorem holds for Lorentzian manifolds too, but the flat metric is now $ \eta_{\mu \nu} $ and its symmetry group is $ \SOn{1, n-1} $. The condition $ g_{\mu \nu , \rho}(p) = 0 $ implies that $ \Gamma^\mu_{\nu \rho}(p) = 0 $, but generally the Christoffel symbols won't vanish away from $ p $. Note, however, that it's not generally possible to ensure the vanishing of second derivatives too: indeed, $ g_{\mu \nu , \rho \sigma}(p) = 0 $ would put $ \frac{1}{4} n^2 (n + 1)^2 $ constraints, but the independent $ \frac{\pa^3 y}{\pa^3 x} $ terms are $ \frac{1}{6} n^2 (n+1) (n+2) $, thus leaving $ \frac{1}{12} n^2 (n^2 - 1) $ free terms: this is precisely the number of independent components of the Riemann tensor, therefore in general it is not possible to pick coordinates as to make the Riemann tensor vanish too.

\subsubsection{Exponential map}

\begin{figure}
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width = 0.70 \textwidth]{exp-map.png}
  \caption{Visualization of the exponential map.}
  \label{fig:exp-map}
\end{figure}

A simple way to construct normal coordinates is the following: given a tangent vector $ X_p \in T_p \mathcal{M} $, there is a unique affinely parametrized geodesic through $ p $ with tangent vector $ X_p $ at $ p $; then, any point $ q $ in the neighbourhood of $ p $ is labelled by the coordinates of the geodesic that takes from $ p $ to $ q $ a fixed amount of time.

Analytically, introducing a coordinate system (not necessarily normal) $ \tilde{x}^\mu $ in the neighbourhood of $ p $, an affinely parametrized geodesic solves \eref{eq:geodesic-equation}, with initial conditions $ \frac{\pa \tilde{x}^\mu}{\pa \tau}\vert_{\tau = 0} = \tilde{X}_p^\mu $ and $ \tilde{x}^\mu (\tau = 0) = 0 $ that make the solution unique. The uniqueness of the solution allows to define a map $ \Exp : T_p \mathcal{M} \rightarrow \mathcal{M} $, called the \bctxt{exponential map}, which acts as follows: given $ X_p \in T_p \mathcal{M} $, construct the appropriate geodesic as above and follow it for a fixed affine distance, conventionally $ \tau = 1 $, to get a new point $ q \in \mathcal{M} $. See Fig. \figref{fig:exp-map} for visual aid. Obviously, there may be points which cannot be reached from $ p $ by geodesics, or there may be tangent vectors $ X_p $ for which $ \Exp $ is ill-defined: in General Relativity, this occurs when spacetime has singularities, but these are not relevant issues.

Pick a basis $ \{e_\mu\} $ of $ T_p \mathcal{M} $. Then $ \Exp : T_p \mathcal{M} \ni X^\mu e_\mu \mapsto q \in \mathcal{M} $, thus it is possible to assign coordinates in the neighbourhood of $ p $ such that $ x^\mu(q) = X^\mu $: these are the normal coordinates. To show this, note that if $ \{e_\mu\} $ is orthonormal, then the geodesics will point in orthogonal directions, ensuring that $ g_{\mu \nu}(p) = \delta_{\mu \nu} $. Now, fix a point $ q $ associated to a given tangent vector $ X_p \in T_p \mathcal{M} $: this means that $ q $ is at distance $ \tau = 1 $ from $ p $ along the given geodesic. Note that the geodesic equation is homogeneous in $ \tau $, thus in general $ \Exp : \tau X_p \mapsto x^\mu(\tau) = \tau X^\mu $, which means that geodesics take a simple form in these coordinates:
\begin{equation*}
  x^\mu (\tau) = \tau X^\mu
\end{equation*}
Being these geodesics, they must solve \eref{eq:geodesic-equation}, that is:
\begin{equation*}
  \Gamma^\mu_{\nu \rho}(x(\tau)) X^\nu X^\rho = 0
\end{equation*}
which holds at any point along the geodesic, i.e. at any $ \tau \in \R^+ $. At most points $ x(\tau) $, this equation only holds for those choices of $ X^\mu $ tangent to the geodesics. However, at $ x(0) = 0 $, i.e. at $ p $, it must hold for any tangent vector: this means that $ \Gamma^\mu_{(\nu \rho)}(p) = 0 $ which, for a torsion-free connection, ensures that $ \Gamma^\mu_{\nu \rho}(p) = 0 $. But vanishing Christoffel symbols imply a vanishing first derivative of the metric: for the Levi--Civita connection $ 2g_{\mu \sigma} \Gamma^\sigma_{\nu \rho} = g_{\mu \nu, \rho} + g_{\mu \rho, \nu} - g_{\nu \rho, \mu} $, thus symmetrizing $ (\mu \nu) $ cancels the last two terms, leaving an identity that, evaluated at $ p $, gives $ g_{\mu \nu, \rho}(p) = 0 $. Hence, these are indeed normal coordinates.

\subsubsection{Equivalence principle}

Normal coordinates are conceptually important in General Relativity: an observer at point $ p $ who parametrizes their immediate surroundings using coordinates constructed by geodesics will experience a locally flat metric. This is precisely Einstein's equivalence principle: any free-falling observer, performing local experiments, will not experience a gravitational field. The formal definition of free-falling observer is an observer which follows geodesics, while the local lack of gravitational field means $ g_{\mu \mu}(p) = \eta_{\mu \nu} $. In this context, normal coordinates are called \bctxt{local inertial frame}.

To understand what ``local" means, note that there is a way to distinguish whether a gravitational field is present at $ p $: a non-vanishing Riemann tensor. This depends on the second derivatives of the metric, which in general will be non-vanishing. However, to measure the effects of the Riemann tensor, one needs to compare the results of experiments at $ p $ and at a nearby point $ q $: this is a non-local observation.

\subsection{Curvature and torsion}

\begin{figure}[!b]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width = 0.90 \textwidth]{parall-tr.png}
  \caption{Parallel transport along different paths.}
  \label{fig:par-tr}
\end{figure}

With reference to \figref{fig:par-tr}, consider a tangent vector $ Z_p \in T_p \mathcal{M} $ and two vector fields $ X,Y \in \xm : [X,Y] = 0 $, i.e. they are linearly independent. Construct two curves $ \gamma, \gamma' $ as in figure, both leading to a point $ r \in \mathcal{M} $ which, for simplicity, is close to $ p $. It is possible to impose normal coordinates centered at $ p $ such that $ x^\mu = (\tau, \sigma, \dots) $, so that $ X = \frac{\pa}{\pa \tau} $ and $ Y = \frac{\pa}{\pa \sigma} $: then $ x^\mu(p) = (0,0,0, \dots) $, $ X^\mu(q) = (\delta \tau, 0, 0, \dots) $, $ x^\mu(s) = (0, \delta \sigma, 0, \dots) $ and $ x^\mu(r) = (\delta \tau, \delta \sigma, 0, \dots)$, with $ \delta \tau, \delta \sigma $ small.\\
First, parallel transport $ Z_p $ along $ X $ to $ Z_q $, so that $ Z^\mu $ solves:
\begin{equation*}
  \frac{\dd Z^\mu}{\dd \tau} + X^\nu \Gamma^\mu_{\nu \rho} Z^\rho = 0
\end{equation*}
In normal coordinates $ \Gamma^\mu_{\nu \rho}(p) = 0 $, thus $ \frac{\dd Z^\mu}{\dd \tau}\big\vert_{\tau = 0} = 0 $ and the Taylor expansion is:
\begin{equation*}
  \begin{split}
    Z_q^\mu
    &= Z_p^\mu + \frac{\delta \tau^2}{2} \frac{\dd^2 Z^\mu}{\dd \tau^2}\bigg\vert_{\tau = 0} + \smo(\delta\tau^3) \\
    &= Z_p^\mu - \frac{\delta\tau^2}{s} \left[ X^\nu Z^\rho \frac{\dd \Gamma^\mu_{\nu \rho}}{\dd \tau} + \frac{\dd X^\nu}{\dd \tau} Z^\rho \Gamma^\mu_{\nu \rho} + X^\nu \frac{\dd Z^\rho}{\dd \tau} \Gamma^\mu_{\nu \rho} \right]_{\tau = 0} + \smo(\delta\tau^3) \\
    &= Z_p^\mu - \frac{\delta\tau^2}{2} X^\nu Z^\rho \frac{\dd \Gamma^\mu_{\nu \rho}}{\dd \tau}\bigg\vert_{\tau = 0} + \smo(\delta\tau^3) = Z_p^\mu - \frac{\delta\tau^2}{2} \left[ X^\nu X^\sigma Z^\rho \Gamma^\mu_{\nu \rho , \sigma} \right]_p + \smo(\delta\tau^3)
  \end{split}
\end{equation*}
where $ \frac{\dd}{\dd \tau} = X^\sigma \pa_\sigma $. Now, $ Z_q $ needs to be parallely transported along $ Y $ to $ Z_r $, but this time $ \frac{\dd Z^\mu}{\dd \sigma}\big\vert_{\sigma = 0} $ doesn't vanish, in general. From the parallel transport equation:
\begin{equation*}
  \frac{\dd Z^\mu}{\dd \sigma}\bigg\vert_{\sigma = 0} = - \left[ Y^\nu Z^\rho \Gamma^\mu_{\nu \rho} \right]_q = - \left[ Y^\nu Z^\rho X^\sigma \Gamma^\mu_{\nu \rho , \sigma} \right]_p \delta \tau + \smo(\delta\tau^2)
\end{equation*}
The expansions of $ Y^\nu $ and $ Z^\rho $ at leading order multiply $ \Gamma^\mu_{\nu \rho}(p) = 0 $, thus only contribute to higher order terms. Next order in $ \delta \sigma $:
\begin{equation*}
  \frac{\dd^2 Z^\mu}{\dd \sigma^2}\bigg\vert_{\sigma = 0} = - \left[ \left( \frac{\dd Y^\nu}{\dd \sigma} Z^\rho + Y^\nu \frac{\dd Z^\rho}{\dd \sigma} \right) \Gamma^\mu_{\nu \rho} + Y^\nu Z^\rho \frac{\dd \Gamma^\mu_{\nu \rho}}{\dd \sigma} \right]_q = - \left[ Y^\nu Y^\sigma Z^\rho \Gamma^\mu_{\nu \rho , \sigma} \right]_p + \smo(\delta\tau)
\end{equation*}
The complete expansion thus is:
\begin{equation*}
  \begin{split}
    Z_r^\mu
    &= Z_q^\mu - \left[ Y^\nu Z^\rho X^\sigma \Gamma^\mu_{\nu \rho , \sigma} \right]_p \delta\tau \delta\sigma - \frac{1}{2} \left[ Y^\nu Y^\sigma Z^\rho \Gamma^\mu_{\nu \rho , \sigma} \right]_p \delta\sigma^2 + \smo(\delta^3) \\
    &= Z_p^\mu - \frac{1}{2} \Gamma^\mu_{\nu \rho , \sigma}(p) \left[ X^\nu X^\sigma Z^\rho \delta\tau^2 + 2 Y^\nu Z^\rho X^\sigma \delta\tau \delta\sigma + Y^\nu Y^\sigma Z^\rho \delta\sigma^2 \right] + \smo(\delta^3)
  \end{split}
\end{equation*}
Parallel transport along $ \gamma' $ leads to a similar expression (exchange of $ X $ and $ Y $):
\begin{equation*}
  {Z'_r}^\mu = Z_p^\mu - \frac{1}{2} \Gamma^\mu_{\nu \rho , \sigma}(p) \left[ Y^\nu Y^\sigma Z^\rho \delta\sigma^2 + 2 X^\nu Z^\rho Y^\sigma \delta\sigma \delta\tau + X^\nu X^\sigma Z^\rho \delta\tau^2 \right] + \smo(\delta^3)
\end{equation*}
The difference between the parallely transported tangent vectors to leading order is:
\begin{equation*}
  \Delta Z_r^\mu = Z_r^\mu - {Z'_r}^\mu = - \left[ \Gamma^\mu_{\nu \rho , \sigma} - \Gamma^\mu_{\sigma \rho , \nu} \right]_p \left[ Y^\nu Z^\rho X^\sigma \right]_p \delta\tau \delta\sigma + \smo(\delta^3)
\end{equation*}
Recalling that $ \Gamma^\mu_{\nu \rho}(p) = 0 $, it is possible to write:
\begin{equation}
  \Delta Z_r^\mu = - \left[ \tensor{R}{^\mu_{\rho \sigma \nu}} Y^\nu Z^\rho X^\sigma \right]_p \delta\sigma \delta\tau + \smo(\delta^3)
  \label{eq:deviation}
\end{equation}
It would be possible to evaluate the expression at $ r $ too, as it would differ only by higher order terms. Although the calculation was carried in a particular choice of coordinates, \eref{eq:deviation} is a tensor relation, therefore it must hold in all coordinate systems: the Riemann tensor thus determines the path dependence of parallel transport.

\subsubsection{Torsion}

\begin{figure}
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width = 0.50 \textwidth]{torsion.png}
  \caption{Visualization of torsion.}
  \label{fig:torsion}
\end{figure}

Consider two tangent vectors $ X_p,Y_p \in T_p \mathcal{M} $ and a coordinate system $ x^\mu $ such that $ X_p = X^\mu \pa_\mu $ and $ Y_p = Y^\mu \pa_\mu $. If $ p : x^\mu $, as in \figref{fig:torsion} construct $ r,s \in \mathcal{M} $ such that $ r : x^\mu + \varepsilon X^\mu $ and $ s : x^\mu + \varepsilon Y^\mu $, with $ \varepsilon $ an infinitesimal parameter. Now, parallel transport $ X_p \in T_p \mathcal{M} $ along the direction of $ Y_p $ to $ X'_s \in T_s \mathcal{M} $ and $ Y_p \in T_p \mathcal{M} $ along $ X_p $ to $ Y'_r \in T_r \mathcal{M} $; their components will be:
\begin{equation*}
  X'_s = \left( X^\mu - \varepsilon \Gamma^\mu_{\nu \rho} Y^\nu X^\rho \right)
  \qquad \qquad
  Y'_r = \left( Y^\mu - \varepsilon \Gamma^\mu_{\nu \rho} X^\nu Y^\rho \right)
\end{equation*}
Repeating this process, starting from point $ s $ and moving along the direction of $ X'_s $, a new point $ q \in \mathcal{M} $ is determined, with coordinates:
\begin{equation*}
  q : x^\mu + \varepsilon \left( X^\mu + Y^\mu \right) - \varepsilon^2 \Gamma^\mu_{\nu \rho} Y^\nu X^\rho
\end{equation*}
Analogously, starting at point $ r $ and moving along the direction of $ Y'_r $, a new point $ t \in \mathcal{M} $ is determined, with coordinates:
\begin{equation*}
  t : x^\mu + \varepsilon \left( X^\mu + Y^\mu \right) - \varepsilon^2 \Gamma^\mu_{\nu \rho} X^\nu Y^\rho
\end{equation*}
If the connection is torsion-free, then $ q \equiv t $. On the other hand, if $ T^\mu_{\nu \rho} \neq 0 $, the parallelogram fails to close, as in \figref{fig:torsion}.

\subsection{Geodesic deviation}
\label{ssec:geo-dev}

\begin{definition}{Tangent and deviation vector fields}{}
  Given a one-parameter family of geodesics $ \{x^\mu(\tau;s)\}_{s \in \R} $ on a manifold $ \mathcal{M} $, the \bcdef{tangent vector field} and the \bcdef{deviation vector field} are defined as:
  \begin{equation}
    X^\mu \defeq \frac{\pa x^\mu}{\pa \tau}\bigg\vert_s
    \qquad \qquad
    S^\mu \defeq \frac{\pa x^\mu}{\pa s}\bigg\vert_\tau
  \end{equation}
\end{definition}
The meaning of these vector fields is evident: the tangent vector field fixes a particular geodesics (i.e. a particular $ s $) and assigns at each point of the geodesic its tangent vector, while the deviation vector field fixes a particular value of the affine parameter $ \tau $ and assigns at each point with this value a vector which takes to a nearby geodesic (at the same $ \tau $).

\begin{figure}
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width = 0.70 \textwidth]{geo-dev.png}
  \caption{A one-parameter family of geodesics generated by $ X $.}
  \label{fig:geo-dev}
\end{figure}

The family of geodesics sweeps a surface embedded in the manifold, so there is freedom in the choice of coordinates $ s $ and $ \tau $. In particular, it's always possible to pick them so that $ X = \frac{\pa}{\pa \tau} $ and $ S = \frac{\pa}{\pa s} $, in order for them to be linearly independent: $ [X,S] = 0 $, as in \figref{fig:geo-dev}. Consider a torsion-free connection, so that:
\begin{equation*}
  \na_X S - \na_S X = [X,S] = 0
  \quad \implies \quad
  \na_X \na_X S = \na_X \na_S X = \na_S \na_X X + \riem(X,S) X
\end{equation*}
But $ X $ is tangent to geodesics, so from \eref{eq:geodesic-def} $ \na_X X = 0 $ and:
\begin{equation}
  \na_X \na_X S = \riem(X,S) X
\end{equation}
Restricting to an integral curve $ \gamma $ of the vector field $ X $, i.e. $ X^\mu \vert_\gamma = \frac{\dd x^\mu}{\dd \tau} $, the covariant derivative along $ \gamma $ becomes:
\begin{equation*}
  \na_X\big\vert_\gamma = X^\mu\big\vert_\gamma \na_\mu = \frac{\dd x^\mu}{\dd \tau} \na_\mu \equiv \frac{D}{D\tau}
\end{equation*}
Hence, in index notation, the change of the deviation vector along the geodesic is expressed as:
\begin{equation}
  \frac{D^2 S^\mu}{D\tau^2} = \tensor{R}{^\mu _{\nu \rho \sigma}} X^\rho S^\sigma X^\nu
  \label{eq:geodesic-deviation}
\end{equation}
This can be interpreted as the relative acceleration of neighbouring geodesics, and it is determined by the Riemann tensor. Experimentally, such geodesic deviations are called \bctxt{tidal forces}, which are the non-local manifestation of a gravitational field as a curvature of spacetime.
